AML Programme Design for Swiss Regulated Entities

AML & Compliance Whitepaper: Programme Design for Swiss Regulated Entities

This whitepaper provides an overview of anti-money laundering programme requirements for Swiss financial intermediaries, with particular reference to the obligations arising from the Federal Act on Combating Money Laundering (GwG), FINMA supervisory guidance, and the impact of Switzerland's 2024 FATF grey list designation.

1. The Current AML Landscape in Switzerland

Switzerland's designation on the FATF grey list in October 2024 — the first time Switzerland has appeared on this list — represents a significant shift in the regulatory environment. The designation was based on deficiencies identified in AML enforcement across several sectors, including the implementation of beneficial owner identification requirements and the adequacy of ongoing monitoring programmes.

The consequences of the grey listing are both reputational and practical:

  • FINMA has publicly committed to increased supervisory intensity, including more frequent on-site inspections and enforcement proceedings against institutions with documented AML deficiencies.
  • Correspondent banking relationships: Some international correspondent banks have applied enhanced due diligence requirements to Swiss institutions, requiring more detailed documentation of AML programmes.
  • Regulatory pressure to remediate: The Federal Council has published a remediation plan that includes legislative amendments and enhanced FINMA enforcement — meaning regulatory pressure will continue to intensify through 2025 and 2026.

For compliance officers at Swiss financial institutions, the practical message is clear: AML programme quality will face greater scrutiny than at any previous point, and documentation of that quality must be immediately available.

2. Core GwG Obligations for Swiss Financial Intermediaries

Obligation GwG Article Practical Requirement
Customer identification Art. 3 Identify contracting party before or at time of entering into relationship. Document type, number, expiry. FINMA Circular 2016/7 governs remote identification.
Beneficial owner identification Art. 4 Identify natural persons owning/controlling >25% of shares or voting rights. If none, identify senior managing official. Document rationale.
Clarification obligation Art. 6 Conduct enhanced due diligence when purpose or background of a transaction is not evident. Document findings.
Increased due diligence Art. 13 Enhanced measures for PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions, complex structures, unusual transactions. Senior management approval required.
Documentation obligation Art. 7 Document all KYC and AML activities, decisions, and findings in a form that allows reconstruction of due diligence process.
Retention obligation Art. 8 Retain all KYC and AML documentation for 10 years after end of business relationship.
Transaction monitoring Art. 5 Monitor business relationships and transactions to detect unusual activity consistent with money laundering or terrorism financing.
Reporting obligation Art. 9 Report to MROS (Money Laundering Reporting Office Switzerland) when money laundering is suspected. Reporting is mandatory, not discretionary.

3. AML Screening: Best Practices

What to Screen Against

A compliant AML screening programme for Swiss institutions must cover, at minimum:

  • SECO Sanctions: State Secretariat for Economic Affairs — Swiss national sanctions list. Mandatory.
  • UN Security Council Consolidated List: Global sanctions for terrorism and proliferation financing.
  • EU Consolidated List: Relevant for Swiss institutions with EU cross-border business.
  • OFAC SDN List: US Treasury sanctions — relevant for transactions involving USD or US-connected parties.
  • PEP Registries: Identification of politically exposed persons, their first and second-degree family members, and close associates.
  • Adverse media: Screening of news and public sources for negative information indicating financial crime risk.

When to Screen

  • At onboarding: Screen all new customers and their beneficial owners before entering into a business relationship.
  • Ongoing: Rescan existing customer base against updated lists. Minimum weekly for most institutions. Continuous for high-risk customer segments.
  • Event-triggered: Rescan when a material change occurs in the customer relationship (new beneficial owner, significant change in transaction profile).

Managing False Positives

False positive rates in AML screening typically range from 95-99.5% at standard match thresholds — meaning most alerts require review and clearance before they are confirmed as genuine matches. Effective false positive management requires:

  • Risk-based match thresholds: Higher sensitivity (lower threshold) for high-risk customers; standard sensitivity for lower-risk customers
  • Additional data points: Using date of birth, nationality, and address to disambiguate name matches
  • Structured review process: Documented escalation and clearance workflow with decision rationale recorded in audit trail
  • Regular threshold review: Quarterly analysis of false positive rates with threshold adjustment

4. Ongoing Monitoring Requirements

FINMA and the FATF consistently identify ongoing monitoring as one of the most common AML programme deficiencies. Screening at onboarding only is insufficient. An effective ongoing monitoring programme requires:

  • Rescreening: The entire customer base must be rescreened against updated lists at a frequency commensurate with risk. For most institutions, weekly rescreening against updated SECO and PEP lists is the minimum acceptable cadence.
  • Transaction monitoring: Unusual transaction patterns must be identified and investigated. This includes transactions inconsistent with the known customer profile, transactions to/from high-risk jurisdictions, and structuring patterns.
  • KYC refresh triggers: The institution must have documented triggers for refreshing KYC records — both on a scheduled basis (annual for high-risk, 3-5 years for standard risk) and on an event-driven basis (new beneficial owner, address change, change in business activity).
  • Documentation: Every monitoring event, alert, and investigation must be documented in the audit trail. FINMA expects to see not just that monitoring occurred, but that alerts were reviewed, findings were documented, and escalation occurred where appropriate.

5. AML Programme Documentation for FINMA Inspection

A FINMA inspection of an AML programme will typically request:

  • AML policy and procedures documentation
  • Evidence of AML screening at onboarding for a sample of recent customers
  • Evidence of ongoing monitoring: screening logs showing dates, lists, and results
  • Alert management log: alerts generated, reviewed, cleared or escalated, with rationale
  • Suspicious Transaction Reports filed with MROS in the inspection period
  • Enhanced due diligence files for PEP and high-risk customers
  • Staff training records for AML function
  • Third-party vendor agreements for any outsourced AML functions

Institutions with automated, documented AML programmes can typically produce all of this documentation on demand within hours. Institutions with manual processes typically require days of preparation — during which deficiencies may become apparent.
