Section 1

What is KYC in Switzerland?

KYC — Know Your Customer — is the legal and procedural framework through which regulated Swiss organisations verify the identity of their customers, assess the risk they represent, and maintain ongoing documentation of that relationship. It is not a voluntary best practice: it is a statutory obligation, enforced by FINMA, with criminal penalties for non-compliance.

Swiss KYC obligations arise primarily from the Federal Act on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (Bundesgesetz über die Bekämpfung der Geldwäscherei und der Terrorismusfinanzierung, GwG/AMLA). This law has been the foundation of Swiss AML compliance since 1998 and has been revised multiple times to align with FATF Recommendations and EU standards. FINMA enforces KYC obligations for the financial sector; the Money Laundering Reporting Office Switzerland (MROS) receives suspicious activity reports; and cantonal authorities supervise non-financial professionals. For a deeper dive, see the Swiss KYC FAQ or the VerfiX AML Whitepaper.

Switzerland has historically been recognised as a global centre for private banking and financial services, which makes robust KYC not only a legal obligation but a reputational necessity. Swiss financial institutions that fail KYC obligations do not just face regulatory fines — they face FINMA enforcement actions that can include withdrawal of operating licences, public sanctions notices, and criminal referrals for senior management.

KYC is enforced under Swiss criminal law. GwG Article 37 provides that intentional violation of the identification and due diligence obligations constitutes a criminal offence. Financial intermediaries — including individual managers — face fines of up to CHF 500,000 for negligent violations and higher penalties for intentional breaches.

Who must perform KYC in Switzerland?

The GwG applies to all financial intermediaries subject to FINMA supervision. Beyond the financial sector, GwG Art. 2(3) extends obligations to certain non-financial professionals when they act in specified capacities:

  • Banks and securities dealers — full GwG obligations including EDD for all PEP relationships
  • Insurance companies — for investment-linked and life insurance products with surrender value
  • Asset managers and fund managers — including FINMA-licensed independent asset managers
  • Crypto exchanges and VASPs — Virtual Asset Service Providers are financial intermediaries under GwG
  • Money transmitters and payment service providers — including cross-border remittance operators
  • Notaries, lawyers, and real estate professionals — when acting as financial intermediaries. See Real Estate KYC and Legal Services KYC
  • Accountants and fiduciaries — when managing assets or establishing structures for clients
Section 2

FINMA — The Swiss Financial Supervisory Authority

The Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA) is the independent federal authority that supervises banks, insurance companies, stock exchanges, securities dealers, and other financial intermediaries under Swiss law. For KYC purposes, FINMA's most important instrument — beyond its supervisory powers — is FINMA Circular 2016/7, which defines the technical requirements for remote digital identification. See the KYC FAQ for common FINMA compliance questions.

FINMA Circular 2016/7 — Online Identification

FINMA Circular 2016/7, titled "Video and Online Identification," establishes the technical and procedural requirements that Swiss financial institutions must meet when identifying customers through digital channels rather than in person. It defines two permissible methods:

Method 1: Video Identification

A trained human agent conducts identification via a live video call. The agent inspects the identity document in real time, verifies the customer's face against the document, and completes the identification according to a structured protocol. This method requires significant operational overhead but may be appropriate for complex or high-risk onboarding scenarios.

Method 2: Online Identification (Automated)

A fully automated digital process that the customer completes independently, without a human agent. FINMA Circular 2016/7 requires this method to meet four specific technical requirements:

1
Document Verification

Check document authenticity including MRZ validation, security feature inspection, and tamper detection across 4,500+ document types.

2
Biometric Face Match

Compare a live selfie against the document photograph using ISO 30107-3 compliant algorithms at a configurable match threshold.

3
Liveness Detection

Confirm the selfie was captured from a live person — not a photograph, video, or 3D mask — using active or passive anti-spoofing.

4
Immutable Audit Trail

Generate a complete, timestamped verification record covering all four steps, retained for the GwG-required period.

VerfiX implements all four FINMA Circular 2016/7 requirements natively. See the Trust Center for the complete FINMA compliance matrix and the KYC Product Sheet for technical specifications.

FINMA Supervisory Approach

FINMA supervises compliance through annual audits conducted by FINMA-licensed external auditors, targeted on-site inspections, and enforcement proceedings when deficiencies are identified. Following Switzerland's 2024 FATF grey listing, FINMA committed to enhanced supervisory intensity — particularly for AML programme documentation quality, UBO identification, and ongoing monitoring evidence.

A FINMA KYC inspection will typically examine: (1) KYC files for a random sample of customers; (2) ongoing monitoring evidence; (3) alert management records; (4) EDD files for PEP relationships; and (5) KYC refresh processes. Institutions using VerfiX can produce this documentation within one hour for any requested time period. See Banking KYC Solutions for detail on inspection preparation.

Section 3

Swiss AML Obligations Under GwG

The Swiss anti-money laundering framework under GwG imposes a layered set of obligations on financial intermediaries. Understanding these obligations is the starting point for building an effective AML compliance programme. The VerfiX AML & Compliance Whitepaper provides a detailed analysis of each requirement.

1
Customer Identification GwG Art. 3

Financial intermediaries must establish the identity of the contracting party at the start of the business relationship, before executing a transaction, or when suspicion of money laundering arises. For individuals, this means verifying a government-issued identity document and confirming the person is who they claim to be. FINMA Circular 2016/7 defines how this may be done digitally.

Document Verification · KYC FAQ
2
Beneficial Owner Identification GwG Art. 4

For every legal entity, the financial intermediary must identify all natural persons who own or control more than 25% of the company's shares or voting rights — directly or indirectly. If no individual meets this threshold, the senior managing official must be identified. This UBO identification requirement is consistently identified as the most frequently deficient element in FINMA AML inspections.

KYB — Business Verification · KYB FAQ
3
AML Screening — SECO Mandatory GwG Art. 3 / SECO

All Swiss financial intermediaries must screen customers against the SECO (State Secretariat for Economic Affairs) sanctions list — Switzerland's national mandatory sanctions register. Screening against additional lists — UN Security Council, OFAC (US), EU Consolidated List, HM Treasury (UK), and PEP databases — is standard practice for institutions with international business and is increasingly expected by FINMA supervisors.

AML Screening · AML FAQ
4
Ongoing Monitoring GwG Art. 5

Financial intermediaries must monitor customer relationships on an ongoing basis — not just at onboarding. Sanctions lists change daily. A customer who was clean at onboarding may appear on SECO or OFAC lists weeks later. PEP status changes with political appointments and departures. Ongoing monitoring detects these changes systematically, generating documented evidence that the institution's AML programme is functioning continuously.

AML Ongoing Monitoring
5
Documentation and Retention GwG Art. 7 & 8

Every KYC activity must be documented in a form that allows reconstruction of the due diligence process. All KYC documentation must be retained for ten years after the end of the business relationship. This documentation obligation is what FINMA inspectors physically examine during supervisory reviews — and what most institutions find most difficult to produce quickly and completely from manual processes.

Switzerland and the FATF Grey List (2024)

In October 2024, Switzerland was placed on the FATF's "Increased Monitoring" list — colloquially known as the grey list — for the first time. The FATF identified strategic deficiencies in three areas: the effectiveness of beneficial owner identification requirements, the supervision of non-banking financial intermediaries (including VASPs and DNFBPs), and the prosecution and sanctioning of money laundering offences.

The practical consequences for Swiss compliance programmes are significant. FINMA has publicly committed to enhanced supervisory activity. International correspondent banks have applied enhanced due diligence to Swiss counterparts. Institutional clients are requesting more detailed AML programme documentation. For organisations that have relied on informal or manual compliance processes, the grey listing creates a clear and immediate imperative to professionalise. See the AML FAQ for detailed guidance.

Section 4

nFADP — Swiss Data Protection for Identity Verification

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, revDSG) entered into force on 1 September 2023. For organisations conducting biometric identity verification as part of KYC, the nFADP introduces critical obligations that must be addressed before any verification workflow goes live. The nFADP FAQ and the nFADP Whitepaper cover these in detail.

Biometric Data — Now Sensitive Personal Data

Under nFADP Article 5(c), biometric data that uniquely identifies a natural person is classified as sensitive personal data — the highest protection category. Facial photographs used for biometric matching, derived facial feature templates, and voice biometric data all fall within this definition. Processing sensitive personal data requires an explicit legal basis documented before processing begins.

For GwG-regulated financial intermediaries, the statutory identification obligation under GwG Article 3 provides this legal basis — the identification obligation is a statutory requirement that justifies the biometric processing necessary to fulfil it. Non-regulated organisations must rely on explicit consent or legitimate interest (with a documented balancing test).

Key nFADP Requirements for KYC Processes

Data Minimisation (Art. 6)

Process only the minimum biometric data necessary. Delete biometric templates and document images after verification — retain only the compliance record.

DPA Mandatory (Art. 9)

A written Data Processing Agreement with every processor handling personal data is now a legal obligation, not a best practice.

Privacy by Design (Art. 7)

Systems must be configured with the most privacy-protective defaults from deployment. Biometric data deletion at session end must be the default, not an option.

Breach Notification (Art. 24)

Breaches likely to result in risk to data subjects must be reported to the FDPIC within a reasonable timeframe — aligned with GDPR's 72-hour standard in practice.

Records of Processing (Art. 12)

Both controllers and processors must maintain records of processing activities covering purpose, data categories, recipients, and retention periods.

DPIA Required

Large-scale biometric processing requires a Data Protection Impact Assessment. FINMA-regulated institutions deploying KYC biometrics almost always qualify.

Section 5

Identity Verification Methods in Switzerland

Swiss financial intermediaries may identify customers through several FINMA-approved methods, each with different levels of assurance, operational complexity, and fraud resistance.

1. In-Person Identification

The traditional method: the customer presents their identity document in person to a trained staff member, who visually inspects it and records the relevant details. This method satisfies all GwG requirements but is operationally expensive, geographically constraining, and — without biometric comparison — not significantly more fraud-resistant than digital methods. Cost: CHF 20-80 per identification. Time: 15-45 minutes including travel.

2. Video Identification (FINMA Circ. 2016/7 Method A)

A live video call with a trained identification agent who inspects the identity document and verifies the customer's face in real time. FINMA-compliant when conducted according to the Circular's requirements. Requires trained agents, scheduling infrastructure, and quality control. Appropriate for complex or high-value onboarding. Cost: CHF 8-25 per identification depending on provider. Time: 10-20 minutes including scheduling.

3. Online Identification — Automated (FINMA Circ. 2016/7 Method B)

Fully automated digital process: document photograph, OCR and security feature verification, biometric face match, liveness detection, AML screening — all completed by the customer independently in under three minutes. FINMA-compliant when all four Circular 2016/7 requirements are met. The VerfiX KYC module implements this method with processing under three seconds. Cost: CHF 1-4 per verification at standard volume. Time: 2-3 minutes customer time.

4. Swiss eID and EU Digital Identity Wallets

The Swiss eID Act and EU eIDAS 2.0 Regulation are introducing government-issued digital identity credentials — the Swiss eID and the EU Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW). Citizens store verified identity credentials on their mobile device and present them to relying parties. The credential is cryptographically signed by the issuing government, providing the highest available assurance level (eIDAS High). The VerfiX eID Gateway accepts Swiss eID and EUDIW credentials from all 27 EU member states through a single integration.

Section 6

Digital Onboarding — The Swiss Standard

Digital onboarding — allowing customers to complete identity verification from their own device without visiting a branch — has become the default expectation for financial services customers in Switzerland. Banks, fintechs, insurers, and crypto exchanges that require in-person identification for account opening face significant competitive disadvantage.

A compliant digital onboarding flow for Swiss KYC contains five elements: (1) document capture and verification; (2) biometric face match against the document photograph; (3) liveness detection; (4) AML screening; and (5) audit trail generation. When all five are present and implemented to FINMA standard, digital onboarding is legally equivalent to in-person identification and produces a more consistent, more complete compliance record.

What makes digital onboarding fail in practice?

The most common failure modes in Swiss digital onboarding implementations are: (1) Liveness detection absent or insufficiently robust — a liveness check that can be defeated by holding up a photograph; (2) document library incomplete — common for platforms that support only a handful of document types, excluding significant portions of the Swiss resident population; (3) biometric match threshold miscalibrated — too low (fraud through) or too high (excessive false rejections); (4) audit trail not generated per FINMA standard — missing timestamps, incomplete evidence, or inaccessible format for FINMA inspection.

Section 7

Common Swiss KYC Compliance Challenges

Based on FINMA enforcement actions, supervisory publications, and engagement with Swiss financial institutions, the following challenges are the most consistently identified in Swiss KYC compliance programmes:

01
Beneficial owner identification gaps

UBO identification for legal entity customers is the most frequently cited deficiency in FINMA AML inspections. Tracing ownership chains manually through multi-tier structures across jurisdictions is time-intensive and error-prone. Automated KYB with registry integration eliminates this gap.

02
Ongoing monitoring not systematic

Many institutions perform AML screening at onboarding but do not have a systematic process for rescreening the existing customer base when sanctions lists are updated. A customer who was clean at onboarding may be listed months later — and without ongoing monitoring, the institution will not detect it.

03
KYC refresh backlogs

Risk-tiered KYC refresh — annual for PEPs, three-year for standard risk — creates large backlogs when performed manually. Digital refresh workflows allow customers to re-verify in under three minutes from any device, without branch visits.

04
Audit trail quality for FINMA inspection

Manually assembled KYC files — scanned PDFs, email chains, spreadsheet notes — are difficult to produce quickly and consistently for FINMA review. Machine-generated, structured audit trails are complete, consistent, and available within one hour.

05
Cross-border document verification

Swiss financial institutions serve customers from 180+ nationalities. Manual KYC teams may not be familiar with the security features of foreign documents. Automated verification covers 4,500+ document types with consistent accuracy regardless of document origin.

06
nFADP compliance for biometric KYC

The September 2023 nFADP change in status of biometric data catches many institutions without the required legal basis documentation, DPAs, and privacy-by-design configuration. This is a live compliance gap for any institution conducting biometric KYC without having completed a nFADP compliance review.

Switzerland KYC — Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to the most common questions about Swiss KYC obligations, FINMA requirements, and nFADP compliance.

KYC — Know Your Customer — is the legal obligation for Swiss financial intermediaries to verify customer identity before entering a business relationship. Obligations arise from the GwG (Federal Act on Combating Money Laundering), supervised by FINMA. They cover individual identification (Art. 3), beneficial owner identification (Art. 4), documentation (Art. 7), and ten-year retention (Art. 8).

All FINMA-supervised financial intermediaries: banks, securities dealers, insurance companies (investment products), fund managers, asset managers, crypto exchanges (VASPs), money transmitters, leasing companies. Also certain non-financial businesses including notaries, lawyers acting as financial intermediaries, real estate professionals, and accountants under GwG Art. 2(3).

FINMA Circular 2016/7 defines the technical requirements for remote Online Identification in Switzerland. It mandates: document verification with MRZ validation and security feature checks; biometric face matching between a live selfie and the document photograph; liveness detection to prevent spoofing; and an immutable audit trail. Any Swiss institution using digital onboarding must comply.

For GwG entities, the statutory identification obligation provides this basis. nFADP also mandates privacy by design (biometric data deleted after verification by default), written DPAs with all processors, and breach notification to the FDPIC within a reasonable timeframe.
