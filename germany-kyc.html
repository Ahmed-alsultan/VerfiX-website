Section 1

What is KYC in Germany?

KYC — Know Your Customer — is the statutory obligation for German financial institutions and designated non-financial businesses to verify the identity of their customers, assess money laundering and terrorist financing risk, and maintain ongoing documentation of the customer relationship. In Germany it is governed by the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG — German Money Laundering Act), which implements the EU's Anti-Money Laundering Directives and aligns with FATF Recommendations.

Germany is the EU's largest economy and one of Europe's most significant financial centres, which makes robust KYC a commercial and regulatory necessity. German-licensed financial institutions that fail their GwG obligations face BaFin enforcement actions — including formal orders, fines of up to EUR 5 million per violation, and publication of enforcement measures. Repeat or severe violations can trigger licence withdrawal. For context on the Swiss equivalent framework, see the Switzerland KYC Guide.

The German GwG has been substantially revised multiple times to implement successive EU AML Directives. The 4th AMLD (2017) introduced risk-based approaches and strengthened beneficial owner obligations. The 5th AMLD (2020) extended coverage to crypto assets and added requirements for high-risk third countries. The 6th AMLD (2021) harmonised predicate offences and introduced criminal liability for legal entities. Germany is now subject to the EU's new AML Regulation (AMLAR), which will directly apply from 2027 and is supervised by the new EU Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA), headquartered in Frankfurt.

AMLA in Frankfurt: The EU's new Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA), which will directly supervise the highest-risk financial institutions from 2027, is headquartered in Frankfurt. Germany will be at the centre of European AML enforcement. Financial institutions operating in Germany should begin aligning with AMLA's forthcoming supervisory standards now.

Who is subject to German GwG?

The German GwG applies to a wide range of organisations — referred to as Verpflichtete (obliged entities):

  • Banks (Kreditinstitute) — all credit institutions licensed under KWG, including savings banks (Sparkassen) and cooperative banks (Volksbanken, Raiffeisenbanken)
  • Payment institutions and e-money institutions — including fintechs with ZAG (Zahlungsdiensteaufsichtsgesetz) licences
  • Securities firms and investment firms — licensed under WpIG or KAGB
  • Insurance companies — for life insurance and investment products with savings components
  • Crypto asset service providers (CASPs) — under MiCA and GwG § 2 Para. 1 Nr. 1b; see Crypto Compliance
  • Asset managers and fund managers — AIFM and UCITS managers
  • Notaries, lawyers and accountants — when acting as financial intermediaries or structuring transactions
  • Real estate agents — for sales and rental transactions above EUR 10,000; see Real Estate KYC
  • Gambling operators — online gambling licensed in Germany
Section 2

BaFin — The German Financial Supervisory Authority

The Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin) is the Federal Financial Supervisory Authority responsible for supervising banks, insurance companies, securities firms, payment institutions, and other financial services entities in Germany. For AML purposes, BaFin works alongside the Bundesbank (for banking supervision) and, for DNFBP sectors, alongside sectoral supervisory authorities (bar associations, notary chambers, professional bodies).

BaFin's AML Supervisory Approach

BaFin has significantly intensified its AML enforcement activity since 2019 following high-profile cases involving German banks. BaFin now uses a risk-based supervisory approach: firms are assessed against a risk profile, and higher-risk institutions receive more frequent and more intensive supervisory attention. BaFin publishes an annual report on money laundering and terrorist financing risk, which sets supervisory priorities for the following year.

BaFin's key supervisory tools for AML enforcement include: Sonderprüfungen (special audits by BaFin-appointed auditors), Verwaltungsakte (formal administrative orders requiring specific remediation), Bußgelder (administrative fines), and Veröffentlichungen (public publication of enforcement measures). In severe cases, BaFin can appoint a Sonderbeauftragter — a special commissioner installed at the institution to oversee compliance remediation.

BaFin's publicly available Auslegungs- und Anwendungshinweise (AAH — interpretation and application guidance) on the GwG is the essential reference document for German KYC compliance programme design. It covers every GwG obligation with interpretive guidance. Available at bafin.de. VerfiX's implementation aligns with the current AAH edition.

Section 3

German GwG Obligations — What is Required

The German GwG structures AML obligations around a risk-based approach: the intensity of due diligence applied to any customer relationship must be proportionate to the assessed money laundering and terrorist financing risk. This contrasts with a rules-based approach that applies identical measures to every customer. For detailed Swiss GwG comparison, see the Switzerland KYC Guide.

1
Identification of the Contracting PartyGwG § 10 Para. 1 Nr. 1

Before entering into a business relationship or above-threshold transaction, the institution must establish the identity of the contracting party. For natural persons: full name, date of birth, nationality, address, and a government-issued identity document. For legal entities: company name, legal form, registered address, registration number, and details of legal representatives.

Document Verification
2
Beneficial Owner IdentificationGwG § 11

For legal entity customers, the institution must identify the ultimate beneficial owner — any natural person who directly or indirectly holds more than 25% of shares or voting rights, or exercises equivalent control. Germany maintains the Transparenzregister (Transparency Register) for UBO data. Financial institutions must check the register and, where discrepancies exist between register data and information obtained from the customer, escalate.

KYB — UBO Identification
3
Risk Assessment — Simplified, Standard and EnhancedGwG § 14–17

GwG § 14 permits simplified due diligence for demonstrably low-risk customers. Standard due diligence applies to the majority of customers. Enhanced due diligence (§ 15) is mandatory for politically exposed persons, customers from high-risk third countries (EU-listed), correspondent banking relationships, and any relationship where suspicion indicators exist. EDD requires source of funds verification, senior management approval, and enhanced ongoing monitoring.

4
Ongoing MonitoringGwG § 10 Para. 1 Nr. 4–5

Institutions must continuously monitor business relationships: keeping customer information current, screening against updated sanctions and PEP lists, and monitoring transactions for patterns inconsistent with the customer's declared profile. Unusual transactions must be investigated and, where suspicion arises, reported to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU — Zentralstelle für Finanztransaktionsuntersuchungen).

Ongoing AML Monitoring
5
Documentation and RetentionGwG § 8

All customer due diligence measures, their results, and the reasoning behind risk assessments must be documented in a form that allows reconstruction of the process. Records must be retained for five years after the end of the business relationship. BaFin inspectors examine these records during supervisory reviews — completeness and accessibility are critical.

Audit Trail & Documentation
6
Suspicious Activity ReportingGwG § 43–47

When a suspicion of money laundering or terrorist financing arises — from an AML screening match, a transaction anomaly, or any other indicator — the institution must submit a Verdachtsmeldung (suspicious activity report) to the FIU within the legally required timeframe. The institution may not tip off the customer that a report has been made.

AML Screening & Alerts

German Transparency Register (Transparenzregister)

Germany operates the Transparenzregister — a central register of beneficial ownership information for all legal entities registered or operating in Germany. Since 2021, all entities must actively report their UBO data to the register (previously there were exemptions for entities whose UBO data was already in other public registers). Financial institutions must check the Transparenzregister as part of their KYB/UBO identification process and document any discrepancies found between the register and information provided by the customer.

Section 4

AML Obligations in Germany — Screening, Monitoring and Reporting

Germany's AML obligations go beyond customer identification. A functioning AML programme requires ongoing transaction monitoring, systematic screening, and a documented process for investigating and reporting suspicious activity to the FIU.

Mandatory Sanctions Screening

German financial institutions must screen all customers and counterparties against applicable sanctions lists. The primary mandatory list is the EU Consolidated Sanctions List, which includes all EU autonomous measures and UN Security Council obligations. German institutions with US connections must also screen against OFAC SDN. The German domestic sanctions ordinances apply SECO-equivalent restrictions in specific sectors. BaFin expects screening to be performed at onboarding and on an ongoing basis when lists are updated — typically daily.

VerfiX Trust Engine screens against the EU Consolidated List, UN Security Council, OFAC SDN, HM Treasury, and multiple PEP databases simultaneously. See the AML FAQ for detailed coverage information.

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)

German GwG § 1 Para. 12 defines PEPs as natural persons who hold or have held prominent public functions, including heads of state, senior government officials, members of parliament, senior judicial officials, central bank executives, ambassadors, senior military officers, and directors of state-owned enterprises. Family members (spouse, children, parents, siblings) and known close associates are also covered. PEP relationships require enhanced due diligence, senior management approval, source of funds verification, and enhanced ongoing monitoring under GwG § 15.

The German FIU and Suspicious Activity Reporting

Germany's Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU — Zentralstelle für Finanztransaktionsuntersuchungen) is the central recipient of Suspicious Activity Reports (Verdachtsmeldungen). Germany has one of the highest SAR volumes in Europe. The FIU analyses reports, shares intelligence with law enforcement, and provides typologies and red flags to the industry. Financial institutions that fail to file required SARs face significant penalties. The obligation to file is triggered by suspicion — not certainty — so the threshold is deliberately low.

Germany receives over 300,000 Suspicious Activity Reports annually — one of the highest volumes in the EU. BaFin and the FIU both emphasise that under-reporting is an active compliance failure. A functioning AML programme requires not just screening capability but a documented process for investigating screening hits and escalating to SAR. Section 5

VideoIdent and Online-Ident — German Remote Identification Methods

Germany was one of the first EU countries to formally recognise and regulate remote digital identification methods. BaFin published its initial VideoIdent guidance in 2014 — significantly earlier than most EU jurisdictions. Two methods are formally recognised under GwG § 12:

VideoIdent (Video-Ident) — Method 1

VideoIdent requires a live video call between the customer and a trained identification agent. The agent visually inspects the customer's identity document, verifies its security features, reads the machine-readable zone data, and compares the customer's live face against the document photograph. The session is recorded and archived. BaFin's Hinweisschreiben sets detailed technical requirements for image quality, lighting, agent training, and platform security.

VideoIdent is operationally intensive. It requires staffed identification agents available during operating hours (or around the clock for international operations), scheduling infrastructure, agent training and quality management, and session recording and storage. Typical cost: EUR 5-15 per identification. Typical time: 10-20 minutes. Completion rate limited by scheduling friction and operating hours.

Online-Ident (Automated Identification) — Method 2

Online-Ident (also called Automated KYC or eKYC) is the fully automated alternative to VideoIdent. No human agent is required. The customer completes the identification independently: photographs their identity document, takes a selfie with liveness check, and the system verifies the document, performs biometric matching, detects liveness, screens against AML lists, and returns a decision. BaFin recognised Online-Ident under GwG § 12 Para. 1 Nr. 5 when it meets the technical requirements. VerfiX implements compliant Online-Ident processing in under three seconds.

Section 6

The German eID — Online-Ausweis and eIDAS 2.0

Germany introduced the electronic identity function (eID-Funktion) in the Personalausweis (national identity card) in 2010. The eID function allows German citizens to prove their identity online with government-level assurance — the card's NFC chip stores biometric data digitally signed by the German government (Bundesdruckerei). eID authentication uses a six-digit PIN chosen by the cardholder, making it resistant to phishing and credential theft attacks.

eID Technical Architecture

The German eID uses the Extended Access Control (EAC) and Password Authenticated Connection Establishment (PACE) protocols standardised by BSI (Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der Informationstechnik). The eID function on the Personalausweis is activated by the holder at the registration office. eID verification requires: an NFC-capable smartphone (all modern iPhones and Android phones support this), the AusweisApp2 or equivalent eID client application, and the cardholder's six-digit eID PIN.

For relying parties accepting German eID, the eID server infrastructure validates the authentication against the German government's trust anchor. This provides the highest available assurance level — eIDAS High — without requiring a human agent. VerfiX eID Gateway supports German eID acceptance alongside EU Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW) credentials from all 27 EU member states through a single integration.

eIDAS 2.0 and the European Digital Identity Wallet

EU Regulation 2024/1183 (eIDAS 2.0) requires all EU member states to make European Digital Identity Wallets (EUDIW) available to citizens by 2026. The EUDIW will allow citizens to store and present verified credentials — identity data, driving licence, professional qualifications — from their mobile device. Germany is developing its national EUDIW implementation based on the German eID infrastructure.

For organisations accepting German credentials, the transition to EUDIW means accepting credentials through a standardised EU protocol (OpenID4VP/OpenID4VCI) rather than the current eID server architecture. VerfiX eID Gateway handles this protocol translation — organisations accepting German credentials today will continue to accept EUDIW credentials after 2026 through the same integration. Section 7

GDPR Compliance for German KYC

Identity verification in Germany involves processing personal data — and biometric data — subject to GDPR. Germany also has a national data protection law, the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG), which supplements GDPR for specific German contexts. The German supervisory authority network consists of the federal Bundesbeauftragter für den Datenschutz und die Informationsfreiheit (BfDI) and sixteen Landesbeauftragte (state commissioners).

Legal Basis for Biometric KYC Under GDPR

Processing biometric data is a GDPR Article 9 special category processing activity, requiring an explicit legal basis. For GwG-regulated German financial institutions, the statutory identification obligation under GwG § 10 constitutes a legal basis under GDPR Art. 6(1)(c) (legal obligation) and Art. 9(2)(g) (substantial public interest). Non-regulated entities must rely on explicit consent (Art. 9(2)(a)) or another applicable special category basis.

Key GDPR Requirements for German KYC Processes

DPA Mandatory (Art. 28)

Written Data Processing Agreement required with all processors handling personal data before processing begins. Germany has among the most active DPA enforcement in the EU.

Data Minimisation (Art. 5)

Collect only the minimum data necessary for the specific KYC purpose. Biometric templates should be deleted after verification — not retained indefinitely.

Privacy by Design (Art. 25)

Systems must implement technical and organisational measures to integrate data protection from the outset — not as an add-on.

DPIA Required (Art. 35)

Large-scale biometric processing requires a Data Protection Impact Assessment. German DPAs (especially in Bayern and Baden-Württemberg) actively enforce this requirement.

Breach Notification (Art. 33)

Breaches must be reported to the competent Landesbeauftragte within 72 hours if likely to risk rights of data subjects.

No Transfer Issues from CH

Switzerland holds EU GDPR adequacy status. No Standard Contractual Clauses required for Swiss-hosted VerfiX processing of German customer data.

German data protection authorities — particularly Bayern LDA and Hamburg HmbBfDI — are among the most active GDPR enforcers in Europe. Financial institutions processing biometric KYC data must have a DPIA in place before deployment. VerfiX provides DPIA support documentation as part of the enterprise onboarding package. Section 8

Common German KYC Compliance Challenges

Based on BaFin enforcement actions, FATF evaluation findings, and engagement with German financial institutions, the following challenges are consistently identified in German KYC compliance programmes:

01
Transparenzregister discrepancies undetected

German GwG requires financial institutions to check the Transparenzregister for UBO data and to escalate discrepancies. Many institutions do check the register but fail to document the comparison — creating a compliance gap even when the data was actually checked.

02
VideoIdent agent quality and consistency

VideoIdent quality depends heavily on agent training and session-by-session consistency. BaFin has cited inconsistent agent behaviour — failing to check all document security features, accepting poor image quality — as a recurring finding in supervisory reviews.

03
Ongoing monitoring not truly ongoing

Many German institutions perform AML screening at onboarding and then rely on annual or ad-hoc rescreening rather than genuine continuous monitoring. BaFin expects institutions to be able to demonstrate that their sanctions screening reflects current list status — not last quarter's list.

04
PEP identification for domestic German PEPs

International PEPs are typically well-covered by commercial PEP databases. Domestic German PEPs — Bundesland ministers, local government officials, state bank executives — are less comprehensively covered in standard databases. German institutions must supplement commercial PEP screening with domestic political knowledge.

05
GDPR/GwG retention conflict

GDPR data minimisation and GwG 5-year retention create a tension: GDPR says delete data when no longer needed; GwG says retain for 5 years. The resolution is that GwG retention constitutes a legal obligation under GDPR Art. 6(1)(c), which overrides data minimisation for the specific compliance records. Many institutions fail to document this legal basis, creating GDPR compliance gaps.

06
Cross-border group consistency

German subsidiaries of international groups often use group-wide KYC platforms built for US or UK regulatory requirements. These may not fully meet German GwG requirements — particularly on VideoIdent/Online-Ident standards, Transparenzregister obligations, and FIU reporting procedures.

Germany KYC — Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to the most common questions about German KYC obligations, BaFin compliance, and VideoIdent.

KYC — Know Your Customer — is the obligation for German financial intermediaries to verify customer identity, assess risk, and maintain documentation. In Germany, KYC obligations arise from the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG — German Money Laundering Act), supervised by BaFin. Core requirements cover identification of contracting parties (§ 10 GwG), beneficial owner identification (§ 11 GwG), ongoing monitoring (§ 10 Para. 1 GwG), and five-year documentation retention (§ 8 GwG).

BaFin (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht) is the German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority, responsible for supervising banks, insurers, securities firms, payment institutions, and crypto asset service providers. BaFin enforces GwG compliance through on-site inspections, formal supervisory measures, and — increasingly — administrative fines. German-licensed VerfiX integrations must satisfy BaFin's expectations as reflected in GwG, BaFin circulars, and the FATF evaluation of Germany's AML framework.

VideoIdent (also Videoidentifizierung or Video-Ident) is the German term for video-based identity verification — a live video call in which a trained identification agent verifies the customer's identity document and confirms their face in real time. BaFin has formally recognised VideoIdent as a compliant method for remote identification under GwG § 12 Para. 1 Nr. 2. BaFin published detailed technical requirements in its 2017 guidance letter (BaFin-Hinweisschreiben) on Video-Ident. VerfiX supports fully automated Online-Ident as an alternative — faster, cheaper, and equally compliant.

Online-Ident (automated digital identification) is the fully automated alternative to VideoIdent. No human agent is required. The customer photographs their identity document, the system verifies it, and a biometric face match with liveness detection confirms the customer is live. BaFin has recognised automated Online-Ident under GwG § 12 Para. 1 Nr. 5 as an alternative to VideoIdent when it meets the technical requirements: document verification, biometric matching, liveness detection, and audit trail. Online-Ident is faster (under 3 minutes vs 10-20 for VideoIdent), lower cost, and available 24/7.

The German GwG applies to: banks and savings banks (Sparkassen, Genossenschaftsbanken), securities dealers and investment firms, insurance companies (for life products), asset managers and fund administrators, crypto asset service providers (under EU MiCA / GwG § 2), payment institutions, money remitters, accountants and tax advisers, notaries and lawyers (when acting as financial intermediaries), and real estate agents above transaction thresholds. Non-compliance with GwG is subject to administrative fines of up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover for legal entities.

The German eID (elektronischer Personalausweis — ePA) is the digital identity function built into the German national identity card (Personalausweis) and electronic residence permit. Using the eID function via NFC on a smartphone, citizens can prove their identity online with government-level assurance. For KYC, the German eID provides the highest available assurance level under BaFin guidance — equivalent to in-person identification. Under eIDAS 2.0, the German eID will also be accessible via the European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW). VerfiX eID Gateway accepts German eID credentials.

This includes identity documents or copies, beneficial owner declarations, AML screening records, transaction monitoring evidence, and all ongoing monitoring documentation. BaFin inspectors examine these records during supervisory reviews — the format must allow reconstruction of the full due diligence process.
