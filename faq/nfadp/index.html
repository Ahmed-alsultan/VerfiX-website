nFADP FAQ

The nFADP (revised Federal Act on Data Protection, revDSG) entered into force on 1 September 2023. It replaced the 1992 law, substantially aligning Swiss data protection with GDPR. Key changes relevant to KYC: biometric data is now sensitive personal data, and written Data Processing Agreements are mandatory.

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, revDSG, nLPD) entered into force on 1 September 2023, replacing Switzerland's 1992 Federal Act on Data Protection. The revision substantially aligned Swiss data protection law with the EU GDPR while retaining Swiss-specific provisions.

Key changes relevant to identity verification and KYC:

1. Biometric data is now sensitive personal data (Art. 5(c)): Biometric data that uniquely identifies a natural person — facial photographs used for matching, derived templates, voice biometric data — is classified as sensitive personal data. Processing requires an explicit legal basis.

2. Data Processing Agreements are mandatory (Art. 9): Written DPAs with all processors are now a legal requirement, not just best practice.

3. Privacy by design and by default (Art. 7): Systems must be configured with the most privacy-protective defaults.

4. Breach notification (Art. 24): Mandatory notification to the FDPIC within a reasonable timeframe (in practice aligned with GDPR's 72 hours) for breaches likely to result in risk to data subjects.

5. Records of processing activities (Art. 12): Controllers and processors must maintain records of processing activities.

VerfiX was designed with nFADP compliance as a foundation, not as an afterthought.

For GwG-regulated financial intermediaries, GwG Art. 3 (statutory identification obligation) provides the legal basis. For non-regulated entities, explicit consent or legitimate interest (with balancing test) is required. The legal basis must be documented before processing begins and disclosed in the privacy notice.

Under nFADP, processing sensitive personal data — including biometric data — requires one of the following bases:

1. Statutory obligation (Art. 31(2)(a)): Processing required by a legal obligation. For GwG-regulated financial intermediaries, the identification obligation under GwG Art. 3 is the statutory basis. This is the cleanest, most robust basis for regulated entities.

2. Consent (Art. 31(1)): Explicit, informed, freely given, revocable consent. Appropriate for non-regulated entities. Must be genuine — bundling consent with service access is problematic.

3. Legitimate interest (Art. 31(2)(b)): Controller's legitimate interest outweighs data subject's privacy interest. Requires documented balancing test. Generally not recommended as sole basis for large-scale biometric processing.

Documentation requirements: The chosen basis must be identified before processing begins; disclosed in the privacy notice; included in records of processing activities (Art. 12); and for GwG entities, referenced in the DPA.

VerfiX's nFADP Whitepaper provides a full legal basis analysis with a compliance checklist. Enterprise customers receive legal basis guidance as part of onboarding.

nFADP requires data minimisation — biometric data should be deleted at session end by default unless a longer retention period is legally justified. GwG Art. 8 requires compliance records to be retained for 10 years. VerfiX maintains separate retention policies: session-only for biometrics, 10-year for compliance records.

The interaction between nFADP data minimisation and GwG retention obligations creates a layered retention requirement that VerfiX resolves through separate retention policies for different data types.

Biometric processing data (facial photographs, biometric templates): nFADP data minimisation (Art. 6) requires retaining personal data only as long as necessary for the purpose. For biometric data used only for identity matching at onboarding, the purpose is fulfilled once the match is confirmed. VerfiX's default: biometric templates and original document photographs are deleted at session end. This is the most privacy-protective default under nFADP.

Compliance records (verification outcome, extracted identity data, AML result, audit trail): GwG Art. 8 requires retention for 10 years after the end of the business relationship. These records are necessary for GwG compliance purposes and must be retained regardless of the nFADP minimisation principle — the GwG statutory obligation provides the legal basis for the retention.

VerfiX configuration: The platform maintains separate retention policies for each data category. Biometric data: session-only by default, configurable longer for specific justified use cases. Compliance records: 10-year default, configurable to shorter periods where GwG retention is not required.

This layered approach fully satisfies both the nFADP minimisation requirement and the GwG retention obligation simultaneously.

Under nFADP Art. 22, a DPIA (Datenschutz-Folgenabschätzung) is required when processing is likely to result in high risk to data subjects. Large-scale biometric processing for KYC typically requires a DPIA. VerfiX provides security documentation, data flow descriptions, and technical specifications to support DPIA completion.

A DPIA (Datenschutz-Folgenabschätzung / Analyse d'impact sur la protection des données) under nFADP Art. 22 is required when processing is likely to result in high risk to the personality or fundamental rights of data subjects. The obligation applies to both controllers and processors.

When a DPIA is required for VerfiX deployments: Large-scale processing of biometric data — nFADP identifies systematic and large-scale processing of sensitive personal data as a high-risk category. Most enterprise deployments of VerfiX for customer KYC will qualify.

FDPIC guidelines: The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) has published guidance on DPIA triggers and methodology. Organisations should consult FDPIC guidance and their legal counsel to determine applicability.

DPIA process: (1) Describe the processing — what data, for what purpose, how long retained, who has access. (2) Assess necessity and proportionality — could the purpose be achieved with less intrusive means? (3) Identify risks — to data subjects' rights and freedoms. (4) Identify mitigating measures — technical and organisational measures that reduce risk. (5) Consult FDPIC if high residual risk remains after mitigation.

VerfiX supports DPIA completion by providing: security documentation, data flow descriptions, sub-processor list, DPA, and detailed specifications of privacy-by-design measures (data minimisation, encryption standards, access controls). This documentation is available from the VerfiX Trust Center.

nFADP Art. 24 requires notification to the FDPIC within a reasonable timeframe for breaches likely to cause high risk to data subjects. VerfiX commits to notifying affected customers within 72 hours of a confirmed breach. The VerfiX Incident Response Policy details response phases, notification templates, and customer communication procedures.

nFADP Art. 24 requires data controllers to report breaches that are likely to result in high risk to the personality or fundamental rights of data subjects to the Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) within a reasonable timeframe. In practice, the FDPIC has indicated alignment with the GDPR 72-hour standard.

VerfiX's breach notification commitments under the Data Processing Agreement:

Customer notification: VerfiX will notify affected customers within 72 hours of a confirmed breach that is likely to result in risk to data subjects. The notification will include: the nature of the breach, data categories and approximate number affected, likely consequences, and measures taken or proposed.

FDPIC notification: VerfiX will advise affected customers on their own FDPIC notification obligation and cooperate with regulatory investigations.

VerfiX Incident Response Policy (available in Trust Center): Defines severity classification (Critical/High/Medium/Low), response timelines for each severity level, containment and remediation procedures, customer communication templates, and post-incident review process.

For Critical incidents (breach affecting multiple customers, confirmed unauthorised system access): Initial response within 1 hour, executive notification within 2 hours, customer notification within 72 hours.

The Incident Response Policy is reviewed annually and available to enterprise customers and procurement teams on request from the Trust Center.
