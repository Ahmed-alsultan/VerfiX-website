Liveness Detection FAQ

Presentation attack detection, anti-spoofing, and deepfake detection in identity verification.

Liveness detection confirms a selfie is from a live person, not a photograph, video, or 3D mask. FINMA Circular 2016/7 explicitly requires anti-spoofing measures for online identification. Without it, an attacker could use a victim's photograph to pass biometric verification. VerfiX implements active and passive liveness meeting ISO 30107-3 standards.

Liveness detection — formally Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) — is the technical measure confirming a biometric capture is from a real, live person rather than a presentation attack.

FINMA Circular 2016/7 explicitly requires liveness detection as a component of online identification for Swiss financial institutions. Without it, the biometric face match is trivially bypassed: an attacker photographs the victim's identity document, uses the document photo as the selfie, and passes the verification.

VerfiX implements two complementary approaches:

Active liveness (challenge-response): The user is prompted to perform an action — blink, turn their head, smile — difficult to replicate with a static photograph or pre-recorded video.

Passive liveness (video analysis): The system analyses the capture stream for artefacts distinguishing live faces from presentations: moiré patterns on printed photos, screen refresh artefacts on displayed videos, edge irregularities on masks, lighting inconsistencies, and micro-movements characteristic of live faces.

Both methods are used in combination. The liveness result is included in the audit trail alongside the match score. VerfiX's implementation is aligned with ISO 30107-3, the international standard for biometric presentation attack detection.

VerfiX detects: printed photo attacks, screen display attacks (smartphone or monitor replay), 3D mask attacks, silicone mask attacks, video replay, deepfake injection, and AI-generated synthetic face attacks. Each is addressed by specific detection mechanisms within the liveness and fraud intelligence stack.

VerfiX Fraud Intelligence and liveness detection together address the full spectrum of presentation attacks:

Print attacks: A printed photograph of the target held up to the camera. Detected by moiré pattern analysis, paper texture signals, frequency domain analysis, and lack of micro-movement.

Screen display attacks: A video or image of the target displayed on a phone or monitor. Detected by screen refresh artefacts, resolution patterns, colour space characteristics, and temporal inconsistencies in the video stream.

3D mask attacks: A physical 3D model of the target's face. Detected by geometric analysis of facial depth cues, light reflection patterns, skin texture analysis, and unnatural micro-movement patterns.

Silicone mask attacks: Realistic silicone masks. Detected by spectral analysis of skin texture, pore structure analysis, and thermal/reflection characteristics.

Video replay attacks: A recorded video of the target performing the liveness gesture. Detected by temporal consistency analysis, compression artefact detection, and challenge-response unpredictability.

Deepfake injection: A synthetic video stream injected directly into the API bypassing the device camera. Detected by device integrity signals, API call pattern analysis, and frequency domain analysis of the submitted frames.

All detection signals contribute to the Trust Score and are logged in the audit trail.

Yes. VerfiX includes dedicated deepfake detection trained on GAN and diffusion-model-generated faces. It analyses frequency domain artefacts, facial boundary irregularities, temporal consistency, and device signals. High-quality deepfakes are detected through signals invisible to the human eye but characteristic of AI synthesis processes.

AI-generated deepfakes represent a rapidly evolving threat to digital identity verification. Standard biometric matching and even standard liveness detection may be insufficient against high-quality deepfakes, because they replicate both facial appearance and natural motion.

VerfiX deepfake detection capabilities:

Frequency domain analysis: GAN-generated and diffusion-model-generated faces leave characteristic patterns in the image frequency domain not visible to the human eye — arising from regularities in AI generation. These patterns are distinct from those produced by real cameras.

Facial boundary analysis: AI-generated faces have subtle irregularities at boundaries — hairlines, ear edges, teeth, glasses frames. The VerfiX model is specifically trained on these boundary artefacts.

Temporal consistency analysis: For video liveness, frame-to-frame inconsistencies in AI-generated video — compression artefacts, temporal flickering, inconsistent micro-expressions — are detected.

Deepfake injection detection: Attacks that inject a synthetic video stream directly into the API (bypassing device camera) are detected through device integrity checks and API call pattern analysis.

Model updating: Deepfake generation technology evolves rapidly. VerfiX deepfake detection models are retrained regularly on newly generated synthetic faces to maintain effectiveness against current generation techniques.

Fraud IntelligenceLiveness

Active liveness asks users to perform a specific action (blink, turn head) — high security, minor user friction. Passive liveness analyses the natural capture stream without prompting — seamless experience, slightly longer to reach confidence. VerfiX uses both in combination for optimal security and user experience.

Active and passive liveness represent two different approaches to confirming that a biometric capture is from a live person:

Active liveness (challenge-response): The system presents the user with a specific challenge — blink, nod your head, smile, look left. The challenge is randomised to prevent pre-recorded attacks. The system verifies both that the correct action was performed and that the biometric characteristics match. High security because it is difficult to perform the correct action with a static photograph. The trade-off is a slight increase in user friction and completion time.

Passive liveness (transparent): No specific user action is required. The system analyses the natural camera stream during normal selfie capture for signals that distinguish live persons from presentations: depth cues, skin texture, micro-movements, lighting reflections, and frequency domain characteristics. The user experience is identical to a standard selfie — no additional steps. The trade-off is that it takes slightly longer to reach confident verification with high-quality inputs.

VerfiX uses both methods in combination: passive analysis runs continuously during the capture, and active challenges are applied when passive confidence is below threshold or when the risk level of the use case warrants the additional security. This optimises both security (no gap left by using only one method) and user experience (active challenge only when needed).

ISO 30107-3 is the international standard for biometric presentation attack detection, defining test methodologies for evaluating liveness systems against known attack types. VerfiX's liveness detection is aligned with ISO 30107-3. This standard is referenced in FINMA Circular 2016/7 as the baseline for biometric anti-spoofing requirements.

ISO 30107-3 — 'Biometric Presentation Attack Detection, Part 3: Testing and Reporting' — is the international standard published by ISO/IEC defining how presentation attack detection (PAD) systems should be tested and their performance reported.

The standard defines: the classification of presentation attack instruments (PAIs — the objects used to perform attacks, such as printed photographs, video replays, and 3D masks); the methodology for evaluating PAD system performance against each PAI type; and the metrics for reporting results — Attack Presentation Classification Error Rate (APCER), Bona Fide Presentation Classification Error Rate (BPCER), and Average Classification Error Rate (ACER).

FINMA Circular 2016/7 references biometric anti-spoofing requirements aligned with ISO 30107-3 as the technical baseline for Swiss financial institutions conducting online identification.

VerfiX's liveness detection is developed and tested in alignment with ISO 30107-3 methodology. The system is evaluated against the PAI types defined in the standard, and performance metrics are tracked using APCER/BPCER/ACER measures.

Detailed technical security documentation including liveness performance metrics is available to enterprise customers and procurement teams from the VerfiX Trust Center on request.
