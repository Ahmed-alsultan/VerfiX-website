Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD): When and How to Apply It

Complete guide to Enhanced Due Diligence under Swiss GwG: mandatory EDD triggers, PEP relationships, source of funds verification, senior management approval and FINMA documentation requirements.

VerfiX AG 2026-06-19 KYC

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) is the heightened level of customer due diligence required for relationships that carry elevated money laundering or terrorist financing risk. It is not a discretionary enhancement — it is a mandatory requirement whenever specific triggers defined in Swiss GwG Article 15, EU AML Directives, and equivalent national law are met. This guide explains when EDD is required, what it involves, and how to document it for FINMA and BaFin inspection. See Risk-Based Approach guide and PEP Screening guide for related context.

When is EDD mandatory?

EDD is triggered automatically — not by discretion — when any of the following conditions are present:

  • PEP relationships: Any customer who is, or whose beneficial owner is, a Politically Exposed Person. Extends to family members and known close associates. See PEP Screening guide.
  • High-risk third countries: Customers from jurisdictions identified by the EU Commission, FATF, or FINMA as presenting elevated ML/TF risk. The EU high-risk third country list is updated periodically and financial institutions must maintain current awareness.
  • Correspondent banking: Relationships with financial institutions in third countries for correspondent banking services require specific EDD including assessment of the respondent's AML controls, senior management approval, and documented responsibility allocation.
  • Non-face-to-face relationships at high value: Non-face-to-face business relationships above defined thresholds where the identity verification cannot be confirmed through in-person means.
  • Complex or opaque structures: Legal entities with ownership structures of unusual complexity or opacity that cannot be satisfactorily explained — shell company indicators, nominee shareholders, bearer shares, complex trust arrangements.
  • High-value transactions from unexpected sources: Large cash transactions or wire transfers inconsistent with the customer's declared profile or business activity.

What EDD requires in practice

1
Source of funds verification

Document where the specific funds in the transaction come from. For wire transfers: bank statements showing the origin. For business payments: invoices, contracts, or other commercial documentation establishing the legitimate business basis.

2
Source of wealth verification

For high-value or complex relationships: document how the customer accumulated their overall wealth. Employment history, business ownership, inheritance documentation, investment returns. Source of wealth goes beyond the specific transaction to the customer's entire financial profile.

3
Senior management approval

GwG and EU AMLD require that a senior manager — not the relationship officer — approves the establishment or continuation of an EDD relationship. This approval must be documented with the approving manager named and the rationale recorded.

4
Enhanced ongoing monitoring

EDD customers require more frequent rescreening and transaction monitoring than standard risk customers. Typically: annual KYC refresh versus three-year for standard risk; continuous transaction monitoring versus periodic batch review; immediate escalation for any anomaly.

5
Documentation

Every EDD measure — the trigger, the documents obtained, the source of funds verification, the senior management approval, the monitoring schedule — must be documented in the customer file and retained for the required period.

PEP EDD — the highest scrutiny category

PEP relationships require EDD regardless of the transaction amount or customer behaviour. The logic: the position itself creates a structural conflict of interest that elevates corruption risk irrespective of individual conduct. The key EDD elements for PEP relationships are source of wealth documentation (how did this public official accumulate this level of assets?), close monitoring of transaction patterns for government-related financial flows, and annual senior management reconfirmation that the relationship remains appropriate. Source of Funds guide covers verification methods in detail.

Correspondent banking EDD

Correspondent banking EDD — for relationships with foreign financial institutions — requires specific additional steps: assessment of the respondent bank's AML/CFT controls, confirmation that the respondent has applied CDD to its customers for whom the correspondent provides services, written agreement on respective responsibilities, and explicit confirmation that the correspondent will not provide services to shell banks.

Documenting EDD for FINMA inspection

FINMA inspections specifically examine EDD files. Inspectors look for: the documented trigger for EDD, the source of funds documentation obtained, the senior management approval record, evidence of enhanced ongoing monitoring, and KYC refresh frequency. VerfiX Trust Engine flags EDD-required customers automatically based on screening results and risk score thresholds, generating a structured EDD checklist in the audit trail that maps to FINMA inspection expectations. Trust Center contains the complete EDD documentation framework.

The most common EDD failure in FINMA inspections is not that institutions fail to perform EDD — it is that the EDD performed is inadequately documented. Source of funds notes like "customer stated funds come from salary" without underlying verification documentation are not EDD. They are unverified assertions. FINMA expects documentary evidence, not customer self-attestation.
