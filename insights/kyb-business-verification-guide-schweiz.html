KYB Verification — A Complete Guide for Swiss Financial Institutions

Know Your Business verification under Swiss GwG: the five steps from company identification to UBO screening, common challenges, and how to automate the process.

What Is KYB and Why Does It Differ from KYC?

Know Your Business (KYB) is the process of verifying the identity, ownership structure, and legitimacy of a business entity before entering into a financial or commercial relationship with it. While KYC (Know Your Customer) focuses on verifying the identity of an individual, KYB adds a layer of complexity: legal entities are not physical persons, they have owners and directors who may themselves need to be verified, and their corporate structures can span multiple jurisdictions and ownership tiers.

Under Swiss law, the obligation to conduct KYB arises primarily from the Anti-Money Laundering Act (GwG), specifically Articles 3 and 4, which require financial intermediaries to identify the contracting party and the beneficial owner. For legal entities, identifying the beneficial owner means tracing the ownership chain until you reach the natural persons who ultimately own or control the entity — the Ultimate Beneficial Owners, or UBOs.

Legal Requirements Under GwG and FINMA Guidance

Article 4 of GwG requires financial intermediaries to identify the beneficial owner of a legal entity and to document that identification. For companies, the beneficial owner is any natural person who owns or controls more than 25% of the shares or voting rights, directly or indirectly. If no natural person meets this threshold, the senior managing official must be identified as the beneficial owner.

FINMA has issued detailed guidance on how this requirement should be met in practice, including the types of documents that must be collected and the processes that must be followed when the ownership structure is complex or when the beneficial owner cannot be identified with certainty. The guidance distinguishes between simplified due diligence (for lower-risk relationships), standard due diligence, and enhanced due diligence (for high-risk relationships).

For Swiss-registered companies, the Zefix central business register (operated by the Federal Office of Justice) is the primary source for corporate information. For foreign companies, financial intermediaries must rely on the relevant national business registry, which may require translation and authentication.

The Five Steps of KYB Verification

Step 1: Company identification. Verify the legal name, registration number, registered address, and legal form of the entity. For Swiss companies, this is obtained from the Zefix register. For foreign companies, the equivalent national register is used. The registration document must be current — typically dated within 90 days.

Step 2: Ownership structure mapping. Document the complete ownership structure of the entity, tracing all shareholdings until you reach natural persons. For simple structures, this may be a single layer. For holding companies or entities with complex cross-border structures, this may require tracing through multiple levels. The result should be an ownership graph showing all entities and their percentage holdings.

Step 3: UBO identification. Identify all natural persons who hold more than 25% of the shares or voting rights, directly or indirectly. For each UBO, you must collect identity verification — typically a passport or national ID card and proof of address. If the KYB process is integrated with a KYC platform, the UBO identity verification can be handled through the same document verification and biometric matching workflow used for individual customers.

Step 4: Director verification. Identify and verify the directors or managing officers of the entity. Directors may need to provide the same identity documents as UBOs, or a simpler verification may be acceptable depending on the risk profile of the relationship.

Step 5: AML screening. Screen all identified entities and individuals — the company itself, its UBOs, and its directors — against global sanctions lists, PEP registries, and adverse media sources. For high-risk relationships, enhanced due diligence may require additional information about the source of funds or the business purpose of the relationship.

Common Challenges in KYB

Complex ownership chains: Multi-tier ownership structures, particularly those involving holding companies in multiple jurisdictions, can make UBO identification extremely time-consuming manually. Automated registry lookups and graph-based ownership mapping significantly reduce this burden.

Missing or unavailable registry data: Not all jurisdictions make business registry data publicly available in machine-readable format. For some countries, manual retrieval and translation may be required, adding days to the onboarding process.

Nominee shareholders and directors: In some jurisdictions, it is common for companies to use nominee shareholders or directors — individuals who hold shares or directorships on behalf of the actual beneficial owner. Identifying the underlying beneficial owner in such cases requires additional documentation.

Keeping records current: Ownership structures change over time through share transfers, reorganisations, and new investors. Financial intermediaries must have a process for refreshing KYB records periodically, or at minimum when a significant change is detected.

KYB Compliance Checklist for Swiss Financial Intermediaries

  • ☐ Company identification: legal name, registration number, registered address, legal form
  • ☐ Current company extract from relevant registry (dated within 90 days)
  • ☐ Complete ownership structure documented to UBO level
  • ☐ UBOs above 25% threshold identified and verified
  • ☐ Directors identified and verified
  • ☐ AML screening: company, UBOs, and directors against SECO, UN, EU, OFAC, PEP
  • ☐ Risk assessment documented and appropriate due diligence level applied
  • ☐ GwG-compliant audit trail compiled and stored for 10 years
  • ☐ Refresh schedule established for periodic KYB review

Conclusion

KYB is significantly more complex than KYC, but the core principles are the same: verify who you are dealing with, screen them against risk lists, document what you found, and maintain the evidence for the required retention period. The key to making KYB efficient is automation — integrating business registry lookups, ownership structure mapping, UBO identification, and AML screening into a single workflow rather than running each step manually.
