FINMA Circular 2016/7 — A Complete Guide for Swiss Banks

Everything Swiss banks need to know about FINMA Circular 2016/7 on video and online identification: technical requirements, audit trail obligations, data residency, and a compliance checklist.

What Is FINMA Circular 2016/7?

FINMA Circular 2016/7 on video and online identification sets out the conditions under which Swiss financial intermediaries may verify the identity of new clients remotely, without an in-person meeting. Before this circular, Swiss banks were required to meet clients face-to-face or use indirect identification via a notarised document. The circular opened the door to fully digital onboarding — provided that specific technical and procedural requirements are met.

The circular applies to all financial intermediaries subject to the Anti-Money Laundering Act (GwG), including banks, securities firms, fund managers, insurance companies, and payment institutions. If your organisation must comply with GwG and you want to onboard customers remotely, you must comply with Circular 2016/7.

The Two Identification Methods

The circular establishes two methods for remote identification:

1. Video identification: A live video call between a trained staff member and the customer, during which the customer presents their identity document and the agent verifies it in real time. This method requires a human in the loop but has been partially automated using AI-assisted document verification running in parallel to the call.

2. Online identification: A fully automated process in which the customer uploads or captures their identity document, and the system verifies it without human involvement. This is the method used by modern KYC platforms including VerfiX. Online identification requires a higher standard of technological assurance, including biometric matching and liveness detection.

For online identification, FINMA requires that the technology can verify both that the document is genuine and that the person presenting it is physically present (liveness). This rules out simple selfie uploads and requires active or passive liveness detection.

Key Technical Requirements

The circular specifies a number of technical requirements that the identification system must meet. Understanding these is essential for selecting a compliant KYC vendor.

Document verification: The system must be able to read and verify identity documents from the relevant jurisdictions. For Swiss banks with international customer bases, this means support for passports, national identity cards, and driving licences from a wide range of countries. VerfiX supports over 4,500 document types from 190+ countries.

Security feature validation: The system must check security features of the document, not just read the printed data. This includes validating the Machine Readable Zone (MRZ), checking the chip data on ePassports (where applicable), and detecting signs of tampering, printing, or photocopying.

Biometric match: The system must compare a live image of the customer with the photograph on the identity document. The circular does not specify a minimum match threshold, but industry practice and FINMA supervisory guidance point to a threshold of 80% biometric similarity as a minimum for unassisted approval.

Liveness detection: The system must verify that the person is physically present and not presenting a photograph, video replay, or synthetic image. Both active liveness (ask the customer to perform an action) and passive liveness (analyse the video stream for signs of life without requiring user action) are accepted, provided the technology meets current anti-spoofing standards.

Audit trail: Every identification event must be documented with a tamper-proof, timestamped record including the outcome, the evidence captured, and the date and time. This audit trail must be retained for the duration required by GwG — currently ten years.

Procedural Requirements

Beyond the technical requirements, the circular also imposes procedural requirements on the financial intermediary:

Staff training: Staff involved in reviewing flagged cases or overseeing automated identification must be trained in document recognition and fraud detection. Even fully automated systems should have a human escalation path for low-confidence cases.

Quality assurance: The financial intermediary must conduct periodic quality checks on the identification system and the outcomes it produces. This includes tracking false acceptance rates (cases where a fraudulent document was accepted) and false rejection rates (cases where a legitimate customer was rejected).

Vendor assessment: If the financial intermediary uses a third-party identification service provider (such as VerfiX), it must conduct appropriate due diligence on the vendor and ensure that a data processing agreement is in place that addresses Swiss data residency, sub-processor management, and audit rights.

Swiss Data Residency Requirement

One requirement that is often underweighted in vendor selection is data residency. FINMA supervisory practice and the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) together create a strong expectation that personal data — and especially biometric data — collected during identification is processed and stored in Switzerland.

While the circular does not explicitly mandate Swiss data residency, FINMA supervisory letters and audit findings have consistently held that financial intermediaries must be able to demonstrate control over where identification data is processed and stored. Using a non-Swiss KYC vendor that processes data in, say, the United States or even the European Union creates a compliance risk, particularly for institutions subject to banking secrecy.

VerfiX operates exclusively in Switzerland, with all data processing and storage occurring in Swiss Tier III certified data centres. This eliminates the data residency compliance risk entirely.

Integration with AML and CDD Requirements

Circular 2016/7 covers the identification of the customer — confirming who they are. But FINMA compliance does not end there. Swiss financial intermediaries must also conduct customer due diligence (CDD) under GwG Art. 3, which includes understanding the purpose of the business relationship, assessing the customer risk profile, and conducting AML screening.

Best practice is to integrate identity verification and AML screening into a single onboarding workflow, so that the identification result and the AML screening result are available together at the point of onboarding decision. VerfiX supports this through the Trust Engine™, which combines identity verification signals with AML screening results into a single risk score and decision.

Common Compliance Gaps

Based on FINMA inspection findings and industry experience, the most common compliance gaps in online identification programmes are:

Insufficient liveness detection: Using a vendor that only requires a static selfie, rather than an active or passive liveness check. This exposes the institution to presentation attacks using printed photographs.

Missing audit trail for rejected cases: Documenting approved identifications but not rejected ones. FINMA expects a complete record of all identification attempts, not just successful ones.

Inadequate vendor due diligence: Selecting a KYC vendor without reviewing their security documentation, sub-processor list, or data residency arrangements.

No quality assurance process: Running automated identification without a periodic review of outcomes and error rates.

Incomplete staff training records: Having trained staff but not documenting the training in a way that can be produced during a FINMA inspection.

FINMA Circular 2016/7 Compliance Checklist

  • ☐ Video or online identification technology selected and validated against circular requirements
  • ☐ Document verification covers all relevant document types and countries
  • ☐ Liveness detection implemented (active or passive)
  • ☐ Biometric match threshold defined and configured
  • ☐ Immutable audit trail with 10-year retention configured
  • ☐ AML screening integrated into onboarding flow
  • ☐ Data processing agreement with KYC vendor in place
  • ☐ Swiss data residency confirmed with vendor
  • ☐ Staff training programme documented
  • ☐ Quality assurance process defined and scheduled

Conclusion

FINMA Circular 2016/7 provides a clear framework for digital customer identification, but the technical and procedural bar is higher than many institutions initially assume. Selecting the right technology vendor, ensuring Swiss data residency, maintaining a complete audit trail, and building a quality assurance process are all essential components of a compliant programme.
