AML Screening Best Practices for Swiss Regulated Entities

How to structure an effective AML screening programme for Swiss regulated entities: sanctions, PEP, adverse media, ongoing monitoring, and false positive management.

What Is AML Screening and Why Is It Required?

AML screening is the process of checking an individual or entity against lists of known sanctioned parties, politically exposed persons (PEPs), and adverse media sources to identify potential money laundering, terrorist financing, or sanctions compliance risks. For Swiss financial intermediaries, AML screening is a legal requirement under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (GwG) and is also required under international standards set by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF).

Switzerland maintains its own sanctions list administered by the State Secretariat for Economic Affairs (SECO). Swiss financial institutions must screen against SECO, and typically also screen against the United Nations Security Council consolidated list, the EU consolidated list, the US Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) list, and the UK HM Treasury list. For institutions with cross-border operations, additional national lists may apply.

The Three Components of Effective AML Screening

Sanctions screening checks whether an individual or entity appears on any government-maintained list of sanctioned parties. Being on a sanctions list means that financial institutions are prohibited from providing financial services to that person or entity, and that assets must be frozen. Sanctions violations carry severe legal and reputational consequences, making accurate and timely screening essential.

PEP screening identifies individuals who hold or have held prominent public positions — heads of state, government ministers, senior judges, central bank governors, senior military officials, and their immediate family members and close associates. PEPs are not prohibited from receiving financial services, but they are considered higher risk because of their greater potential exposure to corruption and bribery. PEP relationships require enhanced due diligence, including understanding the source of wealth and heightened ongoing monitoring.

Adverse media screening involves searching news and other public sources for negative information about a customer that may indicate financial crime risk, even when the person does not appear on any formal list. Adverse media screening is particularly important for identifying individuals who are under investigation but have not yet been formally sanctioned, or who are involved in jurisdictions where formal list coverage is limited.

Screening at Onboarding vs Ongoing Monitoring

Many institutions treat AML screening as a one-time check conducted at the point of onboarding. This is insufficient. Sanctions lists are updated frequently — sometimes daily — and a customer who was clean at onboarding may appear on a list months or years later. FINMA and FATF guidance requires ongoing monitoring of customer relationships, including periodic or continuous rescreening against updated lists.

Ongoing monitoring can be implemented in two ways: batch rescreening, where the entire customer base is screened against updated lists on a scheduled basis (typically daily or weekly), or event-triggered rescreening, where rescreening is triggered by specific events such as a list update affecting a customer's name or jurisdiction, or a significant change in the customer relationship.

The False Positive Problem

One of the most significant operational challenges in AML screening is managing false positives — alerts triggered by name matches that turn out not to be genuine sanctions or PEP matches. A common cause is common names: if a customer shares their name with a sanctioned individual, the screening system will generate a match that must be reviewed and cleared manually.

The industry standard for large financial institutions is a false positive ratio of between 95% and 99.5% — meaning that between 95 and 995 out of every 1,000 alerts are false positives requiring manual review. Reducing the false positive rate without missing genuine matches is one of the primary challenges in AML screening system design.

Effective false positive management depends on several factors: the quality of the matching algorithm, the use of additional data points beyond name (date of birth, nationality, address) to disambiguate matches, configurable match thresholds that allow institutions to tune the sensitivity of the screening, and a structured escalation and clearance process for reviewing alerts.

Configuring Match Thresholds

AML screening systems typically use fuzzy matching algorithms that generate a match score between 0 and 100. The institution must configure a threshold above which a match is flagged for review. Setting this threshold too high reduces false positives but risks missing genuine matches (false negatives). Setting it too low increases the burden on compliance teams managing alerts.

Best practice is to use different thresholds for different risk scenarios. High-risk customers or high-value transactions may use a lower threshold (more sensitive screening) while standard risk customers use a higher threshold (fewer false positives). The VerfiX AML screening API supports configurable match thresholds per screening request, allowing institutions to implement risk-based screening without managing separate systems.

AML Screening Best Practice Checklist

  • ☐ Screen against SECO, UN, EU, OFAC, and HM Treasury at minimum
  • ☐ Include PEP screening with family member and close associate coverage
  • ☐ Include adverse media screening for high-risk customers
  • ☐ Implement ongoing monitoring with at minimum weekly rescreening
  • ☐ Configure match thresholds based on customer risk profile
  • ☐ Document all alerts, reviews, and clearance decisions
  • ☐ Maintain 10-year audit trail of all screening events
  • ☐ Test your screening system against known sanctioned names quarterly
  • ☐ Review false positive rates monthly and adjust thresholds accordingly

Conclusion

Effective AML screening is not a single check — it is a continuous programme combining sanctions, PEP, and adverse media screening with configurable thresholds, ongoing monitoring, and a structured alert management process. The goal is to identify genuine risks quickly and accurately while keeping the operational burden of false positive management manageable.
