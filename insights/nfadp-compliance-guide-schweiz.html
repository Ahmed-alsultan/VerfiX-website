nFADP / Compliance June 2025 · 10 min read

nFADP Compliance Guide for Swiss Organisations

A practical guide to the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection: biometric data obligations, DPIAs, controller-processor requirements, and a compliance checklist.

What Is the nFADP?

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (Bundesgesetz über den Datenschutz, revDSG, commonly referred to as nFADP) entered into force on 1 September 2023. It replaced the Federal Act on Data Protection of 1992, which had become significantly outdated relative to modern data processing practices and international standards. The nFADP aligns Swiss data protection law substantially with the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) while retaining Swiss-specific provisions and maintaining the Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) as the supervisory authority.

The revision matters enormously for any Swiss organisation that processes personal data — which includes virtually every financial institution, KYC provider, and regulated entity in Switzerland. For organisations that process biometric data as part of identity verification workflows, the nFADP introduces specific and significant new obligations.

Biometric Data: Now a Special Category

The most significant change for organisations conducting identity verification is the reclassification of biometric data as a special category of particularly sensitive personal data. Under the old law, biometric data was not specifically classified. Under the nFADP, processing biometric data that allows for the unique identification of a natural person — which includes facial recognition data and voice biometrics used in KYC — now requires heightened justification and documentation.

To process special category data lawfully under the nFADP, an organisation must be able to point to one of the following justifications: explicit consent from the data subject; a legal obligation that requires the processing; the vital interests of the data subject; data that the data subject has manifestly made public; or processing that is necessary for the assertion, exercise, or defence of legal claims.

For most KYC workflows, the relevant justification will be the legal obligation to verify customer identity under GwG (for regulated entities) or the contractual necessity of processing (where the identity check is a prerequisite for opening an account). Organisations must document which justification applies to each type of biometric processing in their records of processing activities.

Privacy by Design and by Default

The nFADP introduces a principle of privacy by design and by default, requiring organisations to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure data protection from the earliest stage of system design and throughout the lifecycle of processing. In practice, this means that identity verification systems must be designed with data minimisation, access controls, and retention limits built in from the start — not added as an afterthought.

For KYC workflows, privacy by design requirements include: collecting only the data necessary for the verification purpose (data minimisation); ensuring that document images are not retained longer than necessary; implementing role-based access controls so that only authorised staff can view identity data; and logging all access to personal data in an audit trail.

Privacy by default means that the default configuration of an identity verification system should be the most privacy-protective setting. If a system can operate in either a data-rich or data-minimal mode, the default should be data-minimal, with the customer or the controller explicitly choosing to enable additional data collection where justified.

Data Protection Impact Assessments

Where processing is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, the nFADP requires organisations to conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) before starting the processing. Biometric processing on a large scale is explicitly identified as a type of processing that is likely to require a DPIA — making this relevant for any financial institution conducting automated KYC at scale.

A DPIA must describe the envisaged processing operations and the purposes, assess the necessity and proportionality of the processing in relation to the purposes, assess the risks to data subjects, and identify the measures envisaged to address the risks. If the DPIA reveals a high residual risk that cannot be mitigated, the FDPIC must be consulted before the processing commences.

Controller-Processor Relationships

The nFADP formalises the obligations of data controllers (organisations that determine the purpose and means of processing) and data processors (organisations that process data on behalf of a controller). For financial institutions that use third-party KYC vendors, this means that a written data processing agreement (DPA) with the vendor is now mandatory — not just best practice.

The DPA must specify: the scope and purpose of the processing; the technical and organisational security measures; the sub-processors used by the processor; the processor's obligations regarding data subject rights requests; and the arrangements for breach notification. Financial institutions should review their existing vendor agreements to ensure they meet nFADP requirements and should request updated DPAs from any vendor that has not yet provided one.

Cross-Border Data Transfers

The nFADP restricts the transfer of personal data to countries that do not provide adequate data protection unless specific safeguards are in place. Switzerland maintains its own adequacy list, separate from the EU adequacy list. Transfers to countries on the Swiss adequacy list (which includes EU/EEA countries, the UK, and a limited number of others) are permitted without additional safeguards.

For biometric data specifically, the heightened sensitivity of the data and the FINMA expectation of Swiss data residency together make processing outside Switzerland difficult to justify. Financial institutions should ensure that their KYC vendors process and store biometric data in Switzerland or in a country with an adequate level of data protection, and that the DPA explicitly addresses the data transfer arrangements.

nFADP Implementation Checklist for KYC Programmes

  • ☐ Records of processing activities (RoPA) updated to include biometric processing
  • ☐ Legal basis for biometric processing documented for each workflow
  • ☐ Privacy notice updated to reflect biometric data processing
  • ☐ DPIA conducted for large-scale biometric processing
  • ☐ Privacy by design review completed for KYC system
  • ☐ Data minimisation: document image retention limited to minimum necessary period
  • ☐ Written DPA in place with all KYC and AML vendors
  • ☐ Sub-processor lists reviewed and documented
  • ☐ Data transfer arrangements confirmed (Swiss residency preferred)
  • ☐ Breach notification procedure documented (72-hour target)
  • ☐ Data subject rights procedure tested end-to-end
  • ☐ Staff training on nFADP obligations completed and documented

Conclusion

The nFADP significantly raises the compliance bar for organisations processing biometric data in Switzerland. The key changes — reclassification of biometric data as sensitive, mandatory DPIAs for large-scale processing, formalised controller-processor relationships, and enhanced breach notification obligations — all require action from financial institutions that have not already updated their programmes.
