FINMA Circular 2016/7 — Online Identification Explained

The complete guide to FINMA Circular 2016/7: what the four technical requirements mean, how to implement compliant Online Identification, and the most common compliance gaps Swiss institutions make.

VerfiX AG 2026-06-19 KYC

FINMA Circular 2016/7 — "Video and Online Identification" — is the technical standard that every Swiss financial institution must meet when identifying customers through digital channels. Published by the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA), it defines exactly what a compliant digital KYC process must include. If you are building or operating a digital onboarding flow in Switzerland, this Circular is not optional reading — it is the regulatory blueprint your system is tested against. See the Switzerland KYC Guide for the full regulatory context.

What does FINMA Circular 2016/7 govern?

The Circular governs remote digital identification — how Swiss financial intermediaries may identify customers without requiring them to appear in person. Before the Circular existed, digital identification had no clear regulatory basis in Switzerland. The Circular formalised two compliant methods and defined the minimum technical requirements for each.

It applies to all FINMA-supervised financial intermediaries: banks, securities dealers, insurance companies (for investment products), asset managers, fund managers, crypto exchanges (VASPs), and payment service providers. Any entity that wants to onboard customers remotely must comply with its requirements.

The two identification methods

FINMA Circular 2016/7 defines two compliant methods for remote digital identification:

  • Method A — Video Identification: A live video call conducted by a trained identification agent who inspects the identity document in real time and verifies the customer's face against it. The session is recorded and archived. Requires scheduled agent availability, training infrastructure, and session storage.
  • Method B — Online Identification (Automated): A fully automated process where the customer completes identification independently. No human agent required. Must meet four specific technical requirements defined in the Circular.

For scale, cost, and conversion rate — Online Identification (Method B) is almost always the right choice for consumer-facing financial institutions. It operates 24/7, costs CHF 1-4 per verification versus CHF 5-15 for video, and achieves higher completion rates because customers complete it at their own pace from any device. VerfiX implements Method B natively.

The four technical requirements for Online Identification

Method B — Automated Online Identification — requires meeting all four of the following requirements simultaneously. Missing any single one means the identification does not meet FINMA standard:

1
Document Verification

The system must capture and verify the identity document. This includes reading and validating the Machine Readable Zone (MRZ), checking document security features (holograms, microprint, kinegrams), confirming the document has not been tampered with, and extracting structured identity data. The document library must cover the customer's identity document type.

2
Biometric Face Matching

The system must perform a biometric comparison between a live selfie and the photograph printed on the identity document (or the photograph on the NFC chip for ePassports). The match must use algorithms that meet the accuracy standard implied by ISO 30107-3. A configurable match threshold determines pass or fail.

3
Liveness Detection

The system must confirm that the selfie was captured from a live, physically present person — not from a photograph, video replay, or 3D mask. This anti-spoofing measure (Presentation Attack Detection) is explicitly required by the Circular. Both active (challenge-response) and passive (video analysis) methods are accepted.

4
Immutable Audit Trail

The entire identification process must be documented in an immutable, timestamped audit record. This record must contain all verification evidence — document images, extracted data, biometric match result, liveness confirmation — and must be retained for the GwG-required ten-year period, accessible for FINMA inspection on demand.

Common compliance gaps

In FINMA supervisory reviews and audits, the most frequently identified gaps in Method B implementations are:

  • Insufficient liveness detection: Liveness checks that can be defeated by holding up a photograph — typically because only passive analysis is used without challenge-response validation. FINMA expects robust anti-spoofing.
  • Incomplete document library: Systems that support only a handful of document types, excluding significant portions of the Swiss resident population (180+ nationalities). The Ausländerausweis and common EU identity cards must be supported.
  • Audit trail not meeting FINMA format: Incomplete records, missing timestamps, or audit trails stored in a format that cannot be quickly exported for FINMA inspection. Machine-generated structured records are significantly more defensible than manual notes.
  • Biometric match threshold not documented: The match threshold used in production must be documented and defensible. FINMA expects institutions to be able to explain why they chose their threshold and what false acceptance rate it implies.

VerfiX implements all four FINMA Circular 2016/7 Method B requirements natively. The complete FINMA compliance matrix — mapping each Circular requirement to the specific VerfiX feature that addresses it — is available in the Trust Center.

NFC chip reading — the optional fifth layer

While not required by FINMA Circular 2016/7, NFC chip reading from ePassports and modern identity cards provides an additional assurance layer that significantly strengthens the identification. The chip's cryptographic data — signed by the issuing government — provides assurance that the document is genuine that even the best visual verification cannot match. For high-value accounts and high-risk customer segments, NFC chip reading is rapidly becoming the expected standard. Read the NFC chip verification guide.
