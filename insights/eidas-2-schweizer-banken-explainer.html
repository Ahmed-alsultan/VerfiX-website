eIDAS 2.0 — What Swiss Banks Need to Know

How the EU Digital Identity Wallet regulation affects Swiss financial institutions: timeline, technical challenge, VerfiX Gateway, and how to prepare.

What Is eIDAS 2.0?

eIDAS 2.0 — formally, Regulation (EU) 2024/1183 — is the revised EU framework for electronic identification and trust services. The original eIDAS regulation of 2014 established mutual recognition of national electronic identity schemes across EU member states, but adoption was limited because not all member states had advanced eID systems, and the schemes that existed were not interoperable at a practical level. eIDAS 2.0 addresses this by mandating that all EU member states offer citizens a European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW) by late 2026.

The EU Digital Identity Wallet is a mobile application that allows EU citizens to store and share verified digital credentials — including their national identity document, driving licence, university diplomas, professional qualifications, and other government-issued attestations — without physically presenting documents. The wallet uses cryptographic signatures to allow the recipient to verify that the credential was issued by a trusted authority and has not been tampered with.

Why eIDAS 2.0 Matters for Swiss Financial Institutions

Switzerland is not an EU member state and is not directly subject to eIDAS 2.0. However, Swiss financial institutions face eIDAS 2.0 obligations from two directions.

EU clients: Swiss banks, asset managers, and other financial institutions with EU-based clients will increasingly be presented with EU Digital Identity Wallet credentials by customers who want to use their wallet for account opening, loan applications, or other financial services. Institutions that cannot accept EUDIW credentials will face friction in their EU client acquisition processes.

EU regulatory compliance for cross-border services: Swiss institutions that provide services to EU clients under equivalence arrangements or through EU branches must comply with EU regulations including AML directives. As EU AML regulation increasingly relies on eIDAS 2.0 as the accepted identity standard for remote identification, Swiss institutions serving EU clients may find that EUDIW acceptance becomes a de facto requirement.

Bilateral digital identity agreements: Switzerland has been in ongoing discussions with the EU regarding digital identity interoperability. The Swiss e-ID Act (enacted in 2024) establishes a national digital identity infrastructure that is designed to be interoperable with eIDAS 2.0, suggesting that Switzerland will seek formal mutual recognition in the coming years.

The eIDAS 2.0 Timeline for Swiss Banks

2024: eIDAS 2.0 regulation published. EU member states begin building wallet infrastructure. Switzerland enacts the e-ID Act.

2025: Pilot implementations of EU Digital Identity Wallets in multiple member states. Early adopter financial institutions begin accepting wallet credentials for digital onboarding. VerfiX Gateway enters early access.

2026: EU member states required to have wallets available to all citizens. Large financial institutions in EU required to accept EUDIW credentials for specified use cases. Swiss banks with significant EU client bases begin experiencing customer demand for wallet-based onboarding.

2027 and beyond: EU AML regulation increasingly references EUDIW as the high-assurance identity standard. Swiss-EU digital identity interoperability discussions reach implementation phase.

The Technical Challenge: 27 Wallet Implementations

The EU Digital Identity Wallet framework specifies interoperability standards, but implementation details vary between member states. Each EU country has its own national wallet system, its own national identity authority, and its own technical implementation. While the credentials are digitally signed and follow common standards (the ARF — Architecture Reference Framework), a financial institution that wants to accept EUDIW credentials from all EU citizens technically faces 27 different integrations.

This is the problem that VerfiX Gateway solves. Rather than building integrations with all 27 national wallet systems, Swiss financial institutions integrate once with the VerfiX Gateway API. VerfiX handles the credential verification, the trust anchor validation, and the cross-jurisdiction interoperability, returning a standardised, verified identity result to the financial institution's onboarding workflow.

Identity Assurance Levels Under eIDAS 2.0

eIDAS 2.0 establishes three identity assurance levels: Low, Substantial, and High. EU Digital Identity Wallet credentials are issued at High assurance level — the highest standard — because they are based on in-person identity proofing by government authorities and use cryptographic signatures that cannot be forged. This is a higher assurance level than most current remote KYC solutions, which typically achieve Substantial assurance.

For FINMA compliance purposes, High assurance EUDIW credentials may satisfy the requirements of FINMA Circular 2016/7 for remote identification, potentially without requiring additional liveness detection or biometric matching — though this interpretation requires confirmation from FINMA as the regulatory framework evolves.

How to Prepare Your Organisation

Swiss financial institutions should begin preparing for eIDAS 2.0 now, even if the immediate regulatory pressure is limited. The steps to take are:

Assess your EU client base. Understand what proportion of your clients are EU citizens who might want to use EUDIW credentials for onboarding or account updates. This will determine the urgency of your eIDAS 2.0 readiness.

Engage your KYC vendor. Ask your current identity verification vendor about their eIDAS 2.0 roadmap. If they do not have a clear plan for supporting EUDIW credential verification, you should evaluate alternatives — including VerfiX Gateway.

Review your legal framework. Work with your legal team to assess what changes to your identification procedures may be needed to accept EUDIW credentials as a valid form of identification under Swiss law and FINMA requirements.

Plan the integration. Even if you are not ready to go live, planning the integration architecture now — including how EUDIW credential data maps to your existing customer data model — will significantly reduce implementation time when you are ready to proceed.

Conclusion

eIDAS 2.0 is not an immediate compliance requirement for Swiss financial institutions, but it is a significant medium-term consideration for any institution with EU clients or cross-border ambitions. The EU Digital Identity Wallet will become the standard identity credential for EU citizens within the next two to three years, and Swiss banks that are not ready to accept it will face competitive disadvantages in EU client acquisition.

VerfiX Gateway is the simplest path to eIDAS 2.0 readiness for Swiss financial institutions — one API integration that handles credential verification across all 27 EU member states.
