FINMA KYC Compliance Checklist for Swiss Banks (2025 Update)

A practical 7-point checklist covering every obligation Swiss financial intermediaries face under GwG, FINMA Circular 2016/7, and the revised nFADP — with a comparison of automated versus manual KYC, and a downloadable PDF.

VerfiX Compliance Team

Introduction 7-Point Checklist Automated vs Manual Download PDF FAQ

Switzerland's regulatory landscape for client identification is one of the most demanding in the world — and in 2025 it became more demanding still. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) placed Switzerland on its grey list in late 2024, citing gaps in the enforcement of anti-money laundering obligations for certain sectors. The Swiss Federal Council and FINMA have responded with increased supervisory intensity, and financial intermediaries across the banking, fintech, insurance, and crypto sectors can expect more rigorous inspection of KYC programmes than at any previous point.

This checklist is designed for compliance officers, risk managers, and senior executives at Swiss financial intermediaries who need to assess and strengthen their KYC programmes quickly. It covers the seven core obligation areas under the Federal Act on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (GwG/AMLA), FINMA Circular 2016/7 on video and online identification, and the revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) as they apply in mid-2025.

Each point includes the legal basis, the practical requirement, the documentation standard, and an indication of where automated KYC technology can reduce burden without compromising compliance.

2025 Context: Following the FATF grey listing, FINMA has signalled increased use of on-site inspections and enforcement proceedings. Institutions with documented, technology-supported KYC programmes are better positioned to demonstrate compliance quickly. Institutions relying on manual, paper-based processes face greater inspection risk.

The 7-Point FINMA KYC Compliance Checklist

1

Legal Basis and Regulatory Scope

Legal basis: GwG Art. 2 (scope), GwG Art. 3 (identification obligation), FINMA Circular 2016/7.

Confirm that your institution has correctly mapped all activities that create GwG obligations. GwG applies to banks, securities firms, fund managers, insurance companies providing life cover, payment institutions, crypto asset service providers, and any entity acting as a financial intermediary under Art. 2 para. 3. If your institution has expanded its product range — for example, adding crypto custody or buy-now-pay-later products — your GwG obligation scope must be updated accordingly.

Documentation required:

  • Written legal opinion or compliance memo confirming GwG scope
  • Product inventory mapped to GwG obligation triggers
  • Annual review by compliance function confirming no scope changes
2

Client Identification at Onboarding

Legal basis: GwG Art. 3, FINMA Circular 2016/7 (remote identification).

Every new client relationship requires identification of the contracting party before or at the time of entering into the relationship. For in-person identification, a current identity document (passport, identity card) must be sighted and recorded. For remote identification under Circular 2016/7, the system must perform document verification (including security feature validation and MRZ check), biometric face matching against the document photograph, and liveness detection. A match threshold of 80% or above is required for unassisted automated approval.

Since nFADP entered into force in September 2023, the collection of facial biometric data during online identification constitutes processing of special category sensitive personal data. This requires a documented legal basis (typically the statutory GwG obligation) and a compliant processing agreement with any third-party KYC vendor.

Documentation required:

  • Timestamped record of identification with document type, number, and expiry
  • Biometric match score and liveness result (remote identification)
  • Signed data processing agreement with KYC vendor confirming Swiss data residency
  • Legal basis for biometric processing documented in records of processing activities
3

Beneficial Owner Identification (UBO)

Legal basis: GwG Art. 4 (beneficial owner), GwG Art. 2a (definition of beneficial owner).

For legal entities, financial intermediaries must identify the natural persons who ultimately own or control the entity. The threshold under GwG is ownership or control of 25% or more of the shares or voting rights. If no natural person meets this threshold, the senior managing official must be identified as the controller. For trusts, foundations, and other non-corporate structures, the beneficiaries and settlors must be identified.

Swiss company registry data from Zefix is the primary source for corporate KYB. For foreign entities, the equivalent national registry must be consulted. Where beneficial ownership is obscured through complex structures or nominee arrangements, enhanced due diligence is required. FINMA expects a full ownership graph that traces to natural persons, not just the immediate legal entity.

Documentation required:

  • Current company register extract (dated within 90 days)
  • Full ownership chain diagram to natural person level
  • Identity verification records for each UBO above 25% threshold
  • Signed Form A (beneficial owner declaration) where applicable
4

Risk Assessment and Client Classification

Legal basis: GwG Art. 6 (due diligence obligations), GwG Art. 13 (increased due diligence), FINMA Circular 2011/1.

Every client must be assigned a risk rating — typically low, medium, or high — based on a documented risk assessment. Risk factors include: the nature of the business relationship, the geographic risk profile of the client and counterparties, the products used, the transaction volumes and patterns, and whether the client or their beneficial owners are politically exposed persons (PEPs). The risk classification drives the intensity of due diligence applied at onboarding and the frequency of ongoing review.

Increased due diligence is mandatory under GwG Art. 13 for clients from high-risk jurisdictions, clients with complex ownership structures, PEPs, and clients for whom the economic background of a transaction is not immediately apparent. Increased due diligence typically requires senior management approval and additional source of funds documentation.

Documentation required:

  • Client risk rating with supporting rationale
  • PEP screening result with date and lists checked
  • Sanctions screening result (SECO, UN, EU, OFAC minimum)
  • Senior approval memo for high-risk relationships
  • Source of funds documentation for increased due diligence cases
5

Documentation, Audit Trail, and Retention

Legal basis: GwG Art. 7 (documentation obligation), GwG Art. 8 (retention), nFADP (retention limits for biometric data).

GwG requires financial intermediaries to document all client identification, due diligence, and AML monitoring activities and to retain that documentation for ten years after the end of the business relationship. The documentation must be sufficient to allow a competent authority to reconstruct the due diligence process, including who performed each step and when.

Under nFADP, biometric data collected during online identification must be handled separately: the identification result and the compliance record must be retained for ten years to satisfy GwG, but the raw biometric data (facial images, biometric template) should be deleted immediately after the session unless there is a specific legal basis for longer retention. Conflating GwG document retention with biometric data retention is a common compliance error.

Documentation required:

  • Immutable, timestamped audit trail for every KYC event
  • Separation of compliance records (10-year retention) from biometric data (session/30 days)
  • Documented retention and deletion schedule
  • Access log showing who viewed KYC records and when
  • FINMA-inspection-ready export capability tested at least annually
6

Ongoing Monitoring and KYC Refresh

Legal basis: GwG Art. 5 (transaction monitoring), GwG Art. 6 para. 2 (updating of identification), GwG Art. 10 (clarification obligation).

KYC is not a one-time event. Financial intermediaries must maintain continuous oversight of existing client relationships and update KYC records when circumstances change. Ongoing obligations include: periodic AML rescreening against updated sanctions and PEP lists; transaction monitoring to detect patterns inconsistent with the known client profile; and triggered KYC refresh when the client's risk profile changes materially (new beneficial owner, change of business activity, unusual transactions).

In 2025, FINMA has specifically noted deficiencies in ongoing monitoring as a systemic weakness across the Swiss financial sector. Institutions that screen clients only at onboarding and not continuously are particularly exposed. Automated ongoing monitoring — where the customer base is rescreened against updated lists daily or weekly — is now effectively best practice for any institution with more than a few hundred clients.

Documentation required:

  • Ongoing AML screening log showing date, lists, and result for each client
  • Transaction monitoring rule set reviewed and updated annually
  • KYC refresh schedule by risk tier (high: annual, standard: 3–5 years)
  • Alert escalation log for triggered refresh events
  • STR (Suspicious Transaction Report) filing log with MROS
7

Penalties, Enforcement, and FATF Grey List Impact

Legal basis: GwG Art. 37 (criminal penalties), FINMAG Art. 31–37 (FINMA enforcement powers).

The consequences of KYC non-compliance in Switzerland are serious and have become more so since the FATF grey listing. FINMA's enforcement toolkit includes: issuing public reprimands (which are disclosed on the FINMA website and carry significant reputational damage); ordering disgorgement of profits derived from non-compliant activities; imposing conditions on or withdrawing authorisation; and banning individuals from senior roles in regulated entities for periods of up to five years.

Criminal penalties under GwG Art. 37 include fines of up to CHF 500,000 for wilful failure to fulfil due diligence obligations. In aggravated cases — for example, where the failure facilitated actual money laundering — criminal charges can be brought against individuals, not just the institution. The FATF grey listing has also created consequences in correspondent banking relationships, with some foreign counterparties applying enhanced scrutiny to Swiss institutions.

For 2025 and beyond, the most effective risk mitigation is a documented, technology-supported KYC programme that can demonstrate compliance quickly during an inspection. Manual programmes with paper-based records are disproportionately exposed to enforcement risk because they cannot produce the structured audit data that FINMA inspectors now expect.

Key actions for 2025:

  • Conduct an internal KYC gap assessment against this checklist
  • Prepare a structured FINMA inspection-ready audit trail export
  • Implement or upgrade ongoing AML rescreening to at least weekly cadence
  • Brief senior management on FATF grey list implications for your institution

Frequently Asked Questions

The primary legal basis for KYC in Switzerland is the Federal Act on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (Geldwäschereigesetz, GwG). Articles 3 and 4 require all financial intermediaries within scope to identify the contracting party and the beneficial owner of any business relationship. FINMA Circular 2016/7 specifies the technical requirements for remote identification (video and online). Since 1 September 2023, the revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) additionally governs the processing of biometric data collected during KYC, classifying it as sensitive personal data requiring a documented legal basis and enhanced safeguards.
Swiss law does not specify a fixed refresh interval. GwG requires that records are kept current based on a risk-based approach. In practice, high-risk clients should be reviewed annually or when a material change occurs in the relationship (new beneficial owner, change in business activity, unusual transactions). Standard-risk clients may be reviewed every three to five years. The critical requirement is that the institution has a documented, risk-based process for triggering KYC refresh, and that this process is functioning and evidenced in the audit trail.
FINMA has broad enforcement powers under the Financial Market Supervision Act (FINMAG), including the issuance of public reprimands (published on the FINMA website), disgorgement of profits, imposition of conditions on or withdrawal of authorisation, and bans on individuals from working in regulated roles for up to five years. Criminal penalties under GwG Art. 37 include fines of up to CHF 500,000 for wilful failure to fulfil due diligence obligations. Following Switzerland's FATF grey list designation, FINMA has signalled significantly increased supervisory intensity, making the cost of non-compliance higher than at any previous point in recent history.
