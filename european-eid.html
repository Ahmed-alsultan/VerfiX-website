Section 1

What is eIDAS?

eIDAS — Electronic Identification, Authentication and Trust Services — is EU Regulation 910/2014, the foundational legal framework governing digital identity and trust services across all 27 EU member states. Adopted in 2014 and in full force from September 2018, eIDAS 1.0 achieved two major goals: it established mutual recognition of national electronic identification (eID) schemes across EU borders, and it created a harmonised legal framework for electronic trust services.

Before eIDAS, digital identity was a national patchwork. A German electronic ID card could not be used to access French government services. A qualified electronic signature issued in the Netherlands had uncertain legal standing in Spain. eIDAS solved this by establishing mutual recognition — an eID accepted in its home member state must be accepted in all other member states for public services at the same or lower assurance level. This cross-border recognition is the foundation on which eIDAS 2.0's much more ambitious vision is built. For the Swiss regulatory context, see the Switzerland KYC Guide.

eIDAS 1.0 — The Three Pillars

Electronic Identification

Mutual recognition of national eID schemes for cross-border public service access. Assurance levels: Low, Substantial, High.

Trust Services

Legal framework for e-signatures, e-seals, timestamps, registered delivery, and website authentication.

EU Trusted Lists

Public registers of Qualified Trust Service Providers (QTSPs) in each member state — the technical backbone of trust.

Section 2

eIDAS 2.0 — EU Regulation 2024/1183

eIDAS 2.0 — formally Regulation (EU) 2024/1183, adopted in April 2024 — is the most significant expansion of European digital identity infrastructure since the original eIDAS regulation. Where eIDAS 1.0 focused on government-to-citizen services, eIDAS 2.0 extends the framework to the private sector, introduces the European Digital Identity Wallet, and raises the ambition from mutual recognition of existing national eIDs to a common, pan-European digital identity infrastructure available to every EU citizen.

The core political commitment of eIDAS 2.0 is that every EU citizen and resident must have access to a European Digital Identity Wallet by 2026. Member states must issue the EUDIW — at no cost to citizens — and it must be accepted by certain categories of private sector relying parties, including financial institutions for KYC purposes, telecoms for SIM registration, and platforms meeting the Digital Services Act's large-platform thresholds.

Section 3

The EU Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW)

The European Digital Identity Wallet is a mobile application — issued by or on behalf of each EU member state — that allows citizens to store, manage, and selectively present verified digital credentials. It is not a single product but a standardised framework: each member state will issue its own national EUDIW implementation, but all will share the same technical standards, credential formats, and presentation protocols, ensuring interoperability across all 27 member states.

EU Digital Identity Wallet — Architecture

What Credentials Can the EUDIW Hold?

The EUDIW is designed to hold a wide range of government and institutional credentials beyond just identity:

  • Person Identification Data (PID) — the core identity credential: name, date of birth, nationality, national ID number, digital photo. Equivalent to the national identity document at eIDAS High assurance.
  • Mobile Driving Licence (mDL) — ISO 18013-5 compliant digital driving licence, accepted as legal equivalent to the physical card in participating jurisdictions.
  • Academic qualifications — university degrees, professional certifications, and diplomas issued by recognised institutions, with cryptographic proof of authenticity.
  • Health credentials — European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) digital equivalent, vaccination records, prescription data.
  • Professional licences — regulated professional qualifications (medical licences, legal qualifications, financial adviser registrations).
  • Social security numbers — tax identification numbers, social insurance identifiers.
  • Bank account information — verified payment account details for instant payment verification.
  • Age attestation — cryptographic proof of age (above a threshold) without revealing date of birth — privacy-preserving age verification for regulated products.

Technical Standards — OpenID4VP and OpenID4VCI

The EUDIW uses two OpenID Foundation protocols as its technical backbone. OpenID for Verifiable Credential Issuance (OpenID4VCI) defines how credentials are issued to the wallet from credential issuers — governments, universities, professional bodies. OpenID for Verifiable Presentations (OpenID4VP) defines how the wallet presents credentials to relying parties — banks, fintechs, government portals — in response to a presentation request.

These protocols are technology-neutral and widely supported. Relying parties integrating VerfiX eID Gateway interact with a single, clean REST API — the protocol complexity of OpenID4VP and the per-member-state trust anchor validation is handled by the Gateway layer. Organisations that build VerfiX eID Gateway integration today will work with all EUDIW implementations across all 27 member states through the same API endpoint. See the eID Gateway Product Sheet.

Selective Disclosure — Privacy by Design

Selective disclosure is one of eIDAS 2.0's most important innovations for data privacy. Traditional identity verification forces the customer to present their entire identity document — containing name, address, photo, document number, expiry date — even when only one attribute is needed. The EUDIW's selective disclosure capability, built on SD-JWT (Selective Disclosure JSON Web Token) and mdoc (ISO/IEC 18013-5) formats, allows the citizen to present only the specific attributes requested for each transaction.

For a financial institution conducting KYC, this means requesting exactly: name, date of birth, nationality, and national ID number — nothing more. The citizen's EUDIW presents a cryptographically bound, government-signed attestation of precisely those four attributes. The institution receives everything it needs for GwG/LCB-FT identification with no data over-collection. This design directly satisfies GDPR and nFADP data minimisation requirements. See the GDPR FAQ and nFADP FAQ for the data protection context.

Section 4

eIDAS Assurance Levels — Low, Substantial, High

eIDAS defines three levels of assurance for electronic identification — Low, Substantial, and High. These levels reflect the confidence that the person claiming an identity is actually who they claim to be. Each level imposes progressively stronger requirements on identity proofing (how the identity was verified initially) and authentication (how the person proves who they are in each subsequent interaction).

Section 5

Cross-Border Identity Verification in the EU

One of eIDAS's foundational achievements — and eIDAS 2.0's expanded ambition — is cross-border identity verification: the ability for a citizen from any EU member state to prove their identity digitally to any relying party in any other member state, with the same legal effect as in their home country. This was technically and legally complex to achieve, because each member state had different national eID systems, different identity document formats, and different legal standards for what constituted valid identification.

eIDAS 1.0 established the principle through notified national eID schemes. eIDAS 2.0 makes it universal through the EUDIW: any EUDIW credential issued by any member state will be cryptographically verifiable against a standardised EU trust anchor, making cross-border verification a technical given rather than a bilateral negotiation. For organisations serving EU customers across multiple member states — a common pattern for Swiss financial institutions, pan-European fintechs, and global insurance companies — this means a single EUDIW acceptance integration covers all EU customer nationalities. VerfiX eID Gateway is exactly this: one integration, 27 member states, all assurance levels.

The Challenge Before eIDAS 2.0

Before eIDAS 2.0, a Swiss bank onboarding EU customers faced a fragmentation problem: German customers might present their Personalausweis NFC chip; French customers their CNIe; Italian customers their CIE; Spanish customers their DNI. Each had different technical standards, different NFC chip protocols, and different issuing authority trust anchors. Supporting all of them required integrating with each member state's eID infrastructure separately — a prohibitive investment for most organisations.

The EUDIW solves this through standardisation. Every member state's EUDIW implementation uses the same presentation protocol (OpenID4VP), the same credential format (SD-JWT or mdoc), and the same trust anchor model (EU Trusted Lists). An organisation that integrates once with this standardised interface accepts all 27 member states' EUDIW credentials through the same code path.

Trust Propagation — How Cross-Border Verification Works

When a citizen presents their EUDIW credential to a relying party (a bank, a government portal, an e-commerce platform), the technical verification works as follows: (1) The relying party's acceptance system sends a presentation request specifying which credential attributes it needs. (2) The citizen's EUDIW wallet selects the appropriate credential and generates a cryptographic presentation containing only the requested attributes (selective disclosure). (3) The presentation is signed by the wallet's private key. (4) The relying party's system validates the presentation signature against the wallet's certificate — issued by the member state. (5) The credential attributes themselves are validated against the issuer's signature — the member state government that issued the PID. Both validation steps rely on certificates from EU Trusted Lists, making the trust chain auditable and publicly verifiable.

Section 6

Trust Services — The Technical Foundation of eIDAS

eIDAS trust services are the technical building blocks that make electronic transactions legally reliable across the EU. They address the fundamental questions of digital transactions: Is this document genuine? Did this person authorise this? Was this document modified after signing? When was this transaction made? Trust services provide cryptographic answers to each of these questions.

eIDAS Trust Services — Service Categories
Electronic Signatures

AdES / QES. Simple, Advanced, Qualified. Legal equivalence to handwritten signatures under eIDAS Art. 25.

Electronic Seals

Legal entity equivalent of signatures. Authenticate documents issued by organisations. QSeal for regulatory submissions.

Time Stamping

Qualified timestamps bind a document to a specific point in time with trusted-third-party attestation. Required for audit trails.

Registered Delivery

Qualified electronic registered delivery services (QERDS). Legal equivalent of registered post for electronic communications.

Qualified Certificates

QC for electronic signatures (QCert/eSign) and seals. Issued by Qualified Trust Service Providers (QTSPs) on EU Trusted Lists.

Preservation Services

Long-term preservation of signed documents beyond the cryptographic validity of original signatures. Required for 10-year AML records.

Electronic Signatures — Simple, Advanced, Qualified

eIDAS defines three levels of electronic signature, each with different legal effect and technical requirements:

  • Simple Electronic Signature (SES) — any data in electronic form attached to or logically associated with other electronic data to sign. Includes a typed name, scanned signature, or click-to-accept. Lowest legal certainty — admissible as evidence but not presumed valid.
  • Advanced Electronic Signature (AdES) — uniquely linked to the signatory, capable of identifying the signatory, created using data under the signatory's sole control, and linked to signed data in a way that detects any change. Digital signatures using PKI certificates. Cannot be repudiated but must prove the connection to the signatory.
  • Qualified Electronic Signature (QES) — an AdES created using a Qualified Electronic Signature Creation Device (QSCD) and a qualified certificate from a QTSP. Has the equivalent legal effect of a handwritten signature in all EU member states under eIDAS Art. 25. Non-repudiable by design.

For financial institutions, QES is relevant for loan agreements, investment mandates, insurance contracts, and any regulated document requiring equivalent legal force to a wet signature. eIDAS 2.0 requires the EUDIW to support QES creation — meaning citizens will be able to sign qualified contracts from their mobile wallet. See the VerfiX Terms of Service for how VerfiX uses electronic signatures in its own contractual processes.

Qualified Certificates and EU Trusted Lists

Qualified certificates are the cryptographic foundation of qualified trust services. They are digital certificates issued by Qualified Trust Service Providers (QTSPs) — organisations that have been assessed against eIDAS technical requirements (ETSI standards EN 319 411, EN 319 412) and granted qualified status by their national supervisory body. Each member state maintains an EU Trusted List (formerly Trusted Service Status List — TSL) identifying all QTSPs in that country and the status of their qualified services.

The EU Trusted Lists are publicly accessible and machine-readable, allowing relying party systems to automatically verify whether a certificate used in a transaction was issued by a currently qualified provider. This public verifiability is what gives eIDAS trust services their cross-border legal reliability — no bilateral trust arrangement is needed because both parties can independently verify the certificate chain against the EU Trusted Lists.

eIDAS 2.0 was adopted in June 2024. The EU Commission established Large-Scale Pilots (LSPs) — four technical pilot projects — to test EUDIW implementation across member states in 2022-2024. These pilots informed the technical specifications. The Implementing Acts defining the detailed technical architecture were published in 2024-2025. Member states are required to make the EUDIW available by the regulatory deadline of 2026.

Section 8

The Future of European Identity Infrastructure

The period from 2025 to 2030 will be the most transformative in the history of European digital identity. Three converging forces — EUDIW deployment, AMLA supervision, and MiCA crypto regulation — will simultaneously raise the floor on identity verification quality while dramatically improving the tooling available to regulated industries.

AMLA — EU Anti-Money Laundering Authority (2027)

The EU Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA), headquartered in Frankfurt, will begin directly supervising the highest-risk financial institutions from July 2027. AMLA represents a fundamental shift: instead of relying entirely on national supervisors (BaFin, ACPR, FINMA) to enforce AML standards, AMLA will directly audit and supervise selected banks, fintechs, and crypto asset service providers across the EU. AMLA's supervisory standards will set the floor for KYC quality across the EU — raising expectations, particularly for digital identification methods and ongoing monitoring evidence.

MiCA and Crypto Asset KYC

EU Regulation 2023/1114 (Markets in Crypto-Assets — MiCA) entered full application in December 2024. All crypto asset service providers (CASPs) operating in the EU must obtain a MiCA licence and comply with full AML obligations equivalent to credit institutions. MiCA creates a single EU passport for crypto services — a CASP licensed in one member state can operate across all 27. KYC requirements under MiCA draw directly on eIDAS assurance levels: EUDIW PID credentials at eIDAS High assurance provide the strongest available KYC evidence for CASP customer onboarding. See the Crypto Compliance page for the full picture.

Convergence — What 2027-2030 Looks Like

By 2028, the European identity landscape will look substantially different from today. Most EU citizens will have access to a EUDIW. The EUDIW will contain their PID, driving licence, and potentially professional credentials. Regulated financial institutions across all 27 member states will be required to accept EUDIW presentations. AMLA will be scrutinising KYC quality at the highest-risk institutions. The combination means: better identity evidence (government-signed, eIDAS High), lower cost (no biometric matching step for EUDIW presentations), and higher regulatory expectations.

Organisations that invest in EUDIW acceptance capability now — 2025-2026 — will enter this environment ahead of their competitors. Those that wait for full 2026 deployment will face a compressed adoption window at exactly the same time their competitors are completing the same integration.

European Digital Identity — FAQ

Common questions about eIDAS, the EU Digital Identity Wallet, electronic signatures, and how VerfiX connects to European identity infrastructure.

eIDAS (EU Regulation No 910/2014 on Electronic Identification and Trust Services) is the EU legal framework that establishes standards for electronic identification, authentication, and trust services across all EU member states. It creates legal recognition for electronic signatures, seals, timestamps, registered delivery services, and website authentication certificates. eIDAS ensures that electronic identification accepted in one member state is recognised in all others for access to public services — the cross-border mutual recognition principle that was eIDAS 1.0's foundational contribution.

eIDAS 1.0 (2014/2016) focused on mutual recognition of national eID schemes for public services and established trust services (electronic signatures, seals, timestamps). It was largely government-to-citizen. eIDAS 2.0 (Regulation EU 2024/1183, adopted June 2024) introduces the European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW) — a citizen-controlled mobile wallet for storing and presenting verified credentials. Key additions: mandatory EUDIW availability for all EU citizens by 2026; extension to private sector relying parties; selective disclosure (sharing only needed attributes); and an expanded credential scope beyond identity (driving licences, diplomas, professional qualifications).

The EUDIW is a standardised mobile application framework that allows EU citizens to store, manage, and selectively present verified digital credentials. It will be issued by EU member states and must be available to all citizens by 2026. Credentials include the Person Identification Data (PID — equivalent to the national ID), driving licence, academic qualifications, professional licences, health insurance card, and bank account information. All credentials are cryptographically signed by the issuing authority. Citizens can present only the specific attributes needed for a transaction — selective disclosure — without revealing unnecessary information.

A Qualified Electronic Signature (QES) is the highest level of electronic signature under eIDAS, carrying the same legal effect as a handwritten signature in all EU member states under eIDAS Article 25. A QES is created using a qualified electronic signature creation device (QSCD) and is based on a qualified certificate issued by a Qualified Trust Service Provider (QTSP) listed on an EU Trusted List. A QES is non-repudiable — the signer cannot subsequently deny having signed. QES is required for high-value contracts, regulated financial agreements, and certain government filings.

A Qualified Trust Service Provider (QTSP) is an organisation that has been assessed by a national supervisory body and placed on an EU Trusted List as qualified to provide trust services under eIDAS. QTSPs are subject to regular audits against eIDAS technical requirements (ETSI standards) and national supervisory oversight. Only QTSPs may issue qualified certificates, provide qualified electronic signature/seal creation services, and operate qualified timestamp or registered delivery services. The EU Trusted Lists are publicly available and regularly updated.

eIDAS 2.0 creates a significant opportunity for financial institutions' KYC processes. When a customer presents their EUDIW Person Identification Data (PID) credential, the institution can verify their identity at eIDAS High assurance level — the strongest available — without requiring a separate document verification and biometric matching step. The credential's authenticity is cryptographically guaranteed by the member state government. AMLD5/6 and national AML laws explicitly permit digital KYC. eIDAS High assurance EUDIW presentation will satisfy the technical requirements of FINMA Circular 2016/7, BaFin VideoIdent/Online-Ident standards, and French CMF Art. L.561-5-1 simultaneously.

Switzerland is not an EU member state and is not directly subject to eIDAS. However, Switzerland has concluded bilateral recognition arrangements with the EU for certain trust services, and the Swiss eID Act (in force 2026) is designed to be interoperable with eIDAS. Swiss organisations wishing to accept EU Digital Identity Wallet credentials from EU citizens can do so through VerfiX eID Gateway — a single integration that handles the cryptographic validation against each member state's trust anchor and returns standardised identity attributes. Switzerland's EUDIW participation is also under active bilateral negotiation.

Selective disclosure allows a EUDIW holder to present only the specific attributes needed for a given transaction — without revealing all the data in a credential. For example, to prove age for an alcohol purchase, the holder presents only a 'date of birth' or 'over 18' boolean — not their full identity document. This privacy-preserving feature uses zero-knowledge proof cryptography and is central to eIDAS 2.0's data minimisation approach. For KYC purposes, financial institutions can request exactly the attributes they need (name, date of birth, nationality, national ID number) without the EUDIW revealing unrelated attributes stored in the same credential.
