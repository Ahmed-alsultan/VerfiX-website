Obligations for Biometric Identity Verification Under Swiss Law

Data Protection and nFADP Compliance in Identity Verification

This whitepaper provides a practical analysis of the data protection obligations arising from the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, revDSG) as they apply to organisations conducting identity verification using biometric technology. It describes how VerfiX AG has designed its platform to support customer compliance with these obligations.

Note: This document is provided for informational purposes only. It does not constitute legal advice. Organisations should consult qualified legal counsel for advice specific to their situation.

1. Background: The nFADP in Context

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (Bundesgesetz über den Datenschutz, revDSG) entered into force on 1 September 2023. It replaced the Federal Act on Data Protection of 1992 and substantially aligned Swiss data protection law with the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) while retaining Swiss-specific provisions.

The revision is significant for identity verification because of two specific changes:

  • ›Biometric data is now classified as a sensitive personal data category (nFADP Art. 5 lit. c), subject to enhanced obligations and requiring explicit justification for processing.
  • ›Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with processors are now mandatory under nFADP, not simply best practice.

For regulated financial institutions conducting KYC using biometric verification, the nFADP creates a compliance programme that must be layered onto their existing GwG and FINMA obligations.

2. Biometric Data Under nFADP

nFADP Art. 5 lit. c defines sensitive personal data to include "biometric data that uniquely identifies a natural person." Biometric data processed in identity verification — including facial photographs used for biometric matching, biometric templates derived from those photographs, and voice biometric data — falls squarely within this definition.

Processing sensitive personal data requires justification. The applicable justifications for biometric KYC data are:

Justification nFADP Basis Application in KYC
Statutory obligation Art. 31 para. 2 lit. a GwG Art. 3 (identification obligation) provides a statutory basis for processing biometric data in the context of legally required KYC. Applicable to GwG-scope entities.
Consent Art. 31 para. 1 Explicit, informed consent from the data subject. Valid for non-GwG entities without a statutory basis. Must be freely given, specific, and revocable.
Legitimate interests Art. 31 para. 2 lit. b Processing is necessary and the controller's legitimate interest outweighs the data subject's interest. Not recommended as sole basis for biometric KYC due to risk of challenge.

Organisations should document the legal basis for each type of biometric processing in their records of processing activities (Article 12 nFADP) before deploying a biometric KYC solution.

3. Privacy by Design and Data Minimisation

nFADP Art. 7 requires that organisations design data processing systems with data protection built in from the outset (privacy by design) and that the default settings of processing systems are the most privacy-protective available (privacy by default).

For biometric KYC, privacy by design requires:

  • ›Processing only the biometric data necessary for the verification purpose — typically the biometric match score, not the raw biometric template or source photograph beyond the minimum retention period
  • ›Automatic deletion of raw biometric data after the verification is complete (unless a longer retention is justified)
  • ›Access controls that prevent biometric data from being accessed by personnel who do not require it
  • ›Documented data flow mapping showing exactly what biometric data is collected, where it is processed, how long it is retained, and when it is deleted

VerfiX implements privacy by design through configurable retention policies that separate compliance records (retained for GwG Art. 8 ten-year period) from biometric processing data (deleted at session end by default).

The interaction between nFADP data minimisation and GwG retention obligations creates a nuanced requirement:

5. Controller-Processor Relationships

When a regulated organisation uses VerfiX to process personal data on its behalf, VerfiX AG acts as a data processor and the regulated organisation acts as data controller. nFADP Art. 9 requires that this relationship be governed by a written Data Processing Agreement.

VerfiX provides a standard DPA to all customers as a baseline. The DPA specifies:

  • ›The scope and purpose of processing
  • ›The technical and organisational security measures VerfiX applies
  • ›The sub-processors VerfiX uses, with a commitment to notify of changes 30 days in advance
  • ›VerfiX's obligations regarding data subject rights requests
  • ›Arrangements for breach notification
  • ›Confirmation of Swiss data residency
  • ›Arrangements for audit rights (one audit per calendar year)

Enterprise customers may request a customised DPA addendum to address specific requirements. Contact privacy@verfix.ch.

6. Cross-Border Data Transfers

nFADP restricts the transfer of personal data to countries that do not provide an adequate level of data protection unless specific safeguards are in place. Switzerland maintains its own adequacy list, separate from the EU list.

VerfiX does not transfer personal data outside Switzerland. All processing and storage occurs within Switzerland. This eliminates the cross-border transfer compliance risk for customers who use the Swiss-hosted cloud or private cloud deployment options.

For on-premise deployments, the customer's own infrastructure policies govern data residency. VerfiX recommends that on-premise customers maintain Swiss data residency for consistency with their overall compliance posture.
