Section 1

What is KYC in France?

KYC — Know Your Customer, or in French Connaissance du Client — is the statutory obligation for French financial institutions and designated non-financial businesses to identify and verify customers, assess their money laundering and terrorist financing risk, and maintain ongoing documentation of the business relationship. In France, KYC obligations arise from the Code monétaire et financier (CMF), specifically Articles L.561-1 through L.574-2, which implement EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives into French law. The framework is commonly referred to as LCB-FT — Lutte contre le blanchiment de capitaux et le financement du terrorisme.

France is one of Europe's largest financial markets and hosts a significant proportion of the EU's banking, insurance, and asset management activity. ACPR, the French prudential supervisor, enforces LCB-FT obligations for regulated financial entities. The AMF (Autorité des marchés financiers) supervises investment services and, jointly with ACPR, crypto asset service providers under MiCA. TRACFIN (Traitement du renseignement et action contre les circuits financiers clandestins) is the national financial intelligence unit. Non-compliance with LCB-FT obligations carries ACPR sanctions of up to EUR 100 million or 10% of annual revenue. Compare with the Switzerland LCB-FT/GwG framework and the German GwG framework.

The French LCB-FT framework has been substantially revised with each successive EU AML Directive. The transposition of AMLD4 in 2017 introduced the risk-based approach and strengthened UBO identification. AMLD5 (2020) extended coverage to crypto assets and added high-risk third-country measures. AMLD6 (2021) harmonised predicate offences. France is now preparing for the EU AML Regulation (AMLAR), which will directly apply from 2027 and be supervised by AMLA — headquartered in Frankfurt.

France's CMF Article L.561-5-1 explicitly permits digital, remote customer identification using electronic identity verification — including automated document verification and biometric matching — when the process meets the technical standards referenced in the regulation. VerfiX implements a fully compliant digital KYC flow under this article. See Document Verification for technical details.

Who must apply LCB-FT KYC in France?

CMF Article L.561-2 defines the entities subject to LCB-FT obligations — referred to as personnes assujetties:

  • Établissements de crédit — banks, savings institutions (Caisses d'Épargne), mutual credit institutions (Crédit Mutuel, Crédit Agricole)
  • Établissements de paiement — payment institutions and e-money institutions licensed by ACPR
  • Prestataires de services sur actifs numériques (PSAN) — crypto asset service providers registered with AMF/ACPR; see Crypto Compliance
  • Entreprises d'assurance — for life insurance and savings products with a cash surrender value
  • Sociétés de gestion de portefeuille — portfolio management companies and fund managers
  • Changeurs manuels — currency exchange businesses
  • Notaires, avocats, experts-comptables — when acting as financial intermediaries; see Legal Services KYC
  • Agents immobiliers — for transactions above CMF thresholds; see Real Estate KYC
  • Opérateurs de jeux — online gambling and casino operators licensed by ANJ
Section 2

ACPR — The French Prudential Supervisory Authority

The Autorité de contrôle prudentiel et de résolution (ACPR) is the French supervisory authority for banking and insurance, operating under the Banque de France. For LCB-FT purposes, ACPR is the competent authority for supervised financial institutions. It conducts on-site inspections (contrôles sur place), off-site reviews (contrôles sur pièces), and enforces compliance through its Commission des sanctions.

ACPR takes a risk-based supervisory approach informed by its annual Analyse sectorielle des risques (sectoral risk analysis) and the national risk assessment (ENRM — Évaluation nationale des risques en matière de blanchiment de capitaux et de financement du terrorisme) conducted every two years. ACPR's supervisory priorities for AML/CFT include: UBO identification gaps, customer risk classification weaknesses, inadequate monitoring of high-risk customers, and deficient SAR (déclaration de soupçon) processes.

ACPR and AMF — Shared Jurisdiction for PSAN

Following EU MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation) application from 2024, crypto asset service providers in France operate under a shared supervisory framework: ACPR supervises prudential and AML requirements; AMF supervises market integrity and investor protection. PSANs must register with AMF under the new MiCA-aligned CASP registration regime and comply with full LCB-FT obligations equivalent to those of credit institutions. Section 3

LCB-FT Obligations — What French Law Requires

The French LCB-FT framework under the CMF imposes a structured set of customer due diligence, screening, monitoring, and reporting obligations. The risk-based approach — approche fondée sur les risques — is central: the intensity of due diligence must be proportionate to the assessed ML/FT risk. For a comparative view, see the KYC FAQ and AML Whitepaper.

1
Identification et vérification du clientCMF Art. L.561-5

Before entering a business relationship or executing an above-threshold transaction, the entity must identify the customer and verify their identity using reliable, independent source documents. For natural persons: full name, date of birth, nationality, address, and an identity document. CMF Art. L.561-5-1 permits digital identification using electronic identity verification when it meets applicable technical standards.

Document Verification
2
Identification du bénéficiaire effectifCMF Art. L.561-5 §2 & L.561-2-2

For legal entity customers, the entity must identify the beneficial owner — any natural person holding more than 25% of shares, voting rights, or other controlling interests, directly or indirectly. France maintains the Registre des bénéficiaires effectifs (RBE). Institutions must consult the RBE and document discrepancies between register data and customer-provided information.

KYB — UBO Identification
3
Évaluation du risque clientCMF Art. L.561-4-1

A risk-based classification is required for every customer: simplified (SDD), standard (SDD standard), or enhanced (EDD). EDD is mandatory for PEPs, customers from high-risk third countries (EU-listed), and complex or atypical relationships. The risk classification must be documented, reviewed periodically, and updated when risk factors change.

Trust Engine — Risk Scoring
4
Mesures de vigilance renforcéeCMF Art. L.561-10

Enhanced due diligence requires source of funds and wealth verification, senior management approval, and enhanced ongoing monitoring. It applies to PEPs (and their family members and close associates), customers from FATF-listed high-risk jurisdictions, correspondent banking, and any relationship presenting significant unexplained complexity or opacity.

AML Screening
5
Surveillance continueCMF Art. L.561-6

Institutions must continuously monitor business relationships: keeping customer data current, rescreening against updated sanctions and PEP lists, and monitoring transactions for patterns inconsistent with the customer's declared profile or risk classification. Unusual operations must be investigated and, where suspicion arises, reported to TRACFIN.

Ongoing Monitoring
6
Conservation des documentsCMF Art. L.561-12

All customer due diligence records must be retained for five years after the end of the business relationship. This includes identity documents or copies, beneficial owner declarations, risk assessments, screening records, and transaction monitoring evidence. ACPR inspectors specifically examine whether records are complete, accessible, and allow reconstruction of the due diligence process.

Audit Trail

Déclaration de soupçon — Reporting to TRACFIN

When suspicion of money laundering, terrorist financing, or corruption arises — triggered by an AML screening match, unusual transaction pattern, or any other indicator — the regulated entity must file a déclaration de soupçon (DS) with TRACFIN via the secure Ermès portal. France receives over 170,000 suspicious activity declarations annually. The obligation is triggered by soupçon — suspicion — not certainty. The entity may not inform the customer that a declaration has been made (interdiction de divulgation). TRACFIN analyses declarations, conducts investigations, and refers serious cases to the parquet national financier (PNF) for prosecution.

ACPR's inspection findings consistently identify deficient SAR processes as a primary compliance gap — specifically: institutions identifying screening matches but failing to investigate them properly, or investigating but failing to file a declaration when the conclusion is suspicious. Section 4

AML Framework — Screening, PEPs and Sanctions

Mandatory Sanctions Screening

French financial institutions must screen customers and counterparties against all applicable EU and UN sanctions lists. The primary mandatory list is the EU Consolidated Sanctions List — which incorporates all EU autonomous restrictive measures and UN Security Council resolutions. French domestic restrictive measures (arrêtés de gel des avoirs) apply national freezing orders issued by the Direction générale du Trésor (DGT). OFAC screening is standard practice for institutions with USD transactions or US correspondent relationships. ACPR expects screening at onboarding and on a continuous basis when lists are updated.

Personnes Politiquement Exposées (PPE/PEP)

CMF Article L.561-10 defines PEPs as natural persons who hold or have held senior public functions: heads of state or government, senior government ministers, members of parliament, judges of supreme courts, senior central bank officials, ambassadors, senior military officers, and directors of state-owned enterprises. French PEPs — including members of the Assemblée nationale, Sénat, and Conseil d'État — must be identified through PEP screening. Family members (conjoint, enfants, parents, frères et sœurs) and close associates are also subject to EDD requirements. VerfiX Trust Engine screens against multiple PEP databases with comprehensive French domestic political coverage.

Registre des bénéficiaires effectifs (RBE)

France's beneficial ownership register — maintained by the Greffe du tribunal de commerce — requires all French legal entities (SAS, SARL, SA, SCI, associations with activities) to register and update their beneficial owner information. Financial institutions conducting KYB must consult the RBE and document any discrepancies between register data and customer-provided information. The RBE is publicly accessible. VerfiX KYB module integrates UBO identification workflows and discrepancy flagging for compliance with this obligation.

Gel des avoirs — Asset Freezing

France operates a national asset freezing mechanism managed by the Direction générale du Trésor (DGT). When a new arrêté de gel des avoirs is issued, financial institutions must freeze assets of listed persons within 24 hours and report the freeze to DGT. This is in addition to EU and UN sanctions obligations and applies to both domestic terrorism-related designations and corruption-related measures. VerfiX ongoing monitoring detects new listings in French, EU, and UN lists and triggers immediate alerts for compliance action.

Section 5

Digital Onboarding in France — CMF Art. L.561-5-1

Digital customer identification (identification à distance) in France is governed by CMF Article L.561-5-1, introduced by the Ordonnance 2020-115 transposing AMLD5. This article expressly permits remote electronic identification when it meets the requirements set by decree — including document verification, biometric comparison, and liveness detection. ACPR and AMF have published joint recommendations (Recommandations conjointes) clarifying the acceptable methods and technical standards.

France has aligned its digital identification standards with eIDAS for high-assurance use cases and with ISO 30107-3 for biometric liveness detection. The acceptable digital identification methods in France range from simple document upload (lowest assurance) to fully automated biometric verification with NFC chip reading (highest assurance) to eIDAS-certified identity providers.

Spécificités françaises de l'identification numérique

Several features of the French digital identification landscape are distinct from other EU markets. First, France has invested significantly in the carte nationale d'identité électronique (CNIe) — the digital national identity card — which includes an NFC chip with biometric data signed by the French Interior Ministry (ANTS). CNIe NFC reading provides the highest assurance level for French citizens. Second, France operates FranceConnect+, the government's high-assurance digital identity federation, which allows citizens to use certified identity providers — including La Poste's digital identity service — to authenticate at eIDAS Substantial assurance level. Third, France is developing its national portefeuille d'identité numérique (digital identity wallet) as its EUDIW implementation, expected to launch progressively from 2025-2026.

Section 6

Electronic Identity Verification — FranceConnect and eIDAS

France has one of the most developed digital identity ecosystems in the EU, centred on FranceConnect. Launched in 2016 and managed by the Direction interministérielle du numérique (DINUM), FranceConnect provides identity federation for French public services — allowing citizens to authenticate using existing government credentials from DGFiP (tax authority), AMELI (health insurance), La Poste, or MobileConnect.

FranceConnect and FranceConnect+

FranceConnect operates at eIDAS Low assurance level — suitable for most public services but insufficient for financial KYC. FranceConnect+ provides eIDAS Substantial assurance through certified identity providers who perform enhanced identity proofing. La Poste's digital identity service is the primary FranceConnect+ provider, having verified identity documents in person during postal operations. For financial KYC, FranceConnect+ provides ACPR-acceptable digital identification that does not require the customer to hold the French CNIe NFC card.

VerfiX eID Gateway integrates with FranceConnect+ to accept identity assertions from certified French identity providers. This allows French financial institutions to accept FranceConnect+ authentication as the KYC identification step — either standalone or as a complement to document verification — through a single API integration.

Carte nationale d'identité électronique (CNIe) — NFC

France began issuing the new biometric national identity card (CNIe) in August 2021. The CNIe includes an NFC chip containing biometric data — facial photograph and fingerprints — digitally signed by the French Interior Ministry through ANTS (Agence nationale des titres sécurisés). Reading and validating the CNIe NFC chip provides eIDAS High assurance level — the strongest available — because the chip data carries a government digital signature that cannot be forged. As of 2025, the majority of new French identity cards issued are CNIe with active NFC function.

For organisations onboarding high-value French customers, CNIe NFC reading provides the strongest available identity assurance while being faster and cheaper than video identification. VerfiX supports CNIe NFC reading through its standard document verification flow. See the eID Gateway Product Sheet for technical specifications.

EUDIW — France's Digital Identity Wallet (2025-2026)

EU Regulation 2024/1183 (eIDAS 2.0) requires all EU member states to make European Digital Identity Wallets (EUDIW) available to citizens by 2026. France is developing its national EUDIW implementation — the portefeuille d'identité numérique — building on the FranceConnect+ and CNIe infrastructure. The wallet will allow French citizens to store and present verified identity credentials, driving licence, and other government-issued attributes from a mobile application, at eIDAS High assurance level. VerfiX eID Gateway already accepts EU Digital Identity Wallet credentials from member states that have deployed them, and will cover France's implementation as it launches.

Section 7

Fraud Prevention — Specificities of the French Market

France faces specific identity fraud challenges that inform KYC and fraud prevention system design. The Observatoire de la sécurité des moyens de paiement (OSMP), operated by the Banque de France, publishes annual fraud statistics for the French market. Key fraud vectors in the French context include document fraud, account takeover through social engineering of banking advisers, and synthetic identity fraud targeting French online financial services.

Document Fraud — French Context

French identity documents have evolved significantly in security over recent years. The new CNIe (2021) has substantially stronger security features than the previous generation carte nationale d'identité. However, the earlier CNI design (valid for up to 30 years for those issued before 2014) remains in circulation with older, more easily forged security features. VerfiX document verification handles both CNI generations, applying the appropriate security feature template for each version and flagging older documents for additional scrutiny based on age and issue date.

Arnaque au faux conseiller bancaire

A distinctive French fraud pattern is the arnaque au faux conseiller bancaire — social engineering attack where fraudsters impersonate bank advisers, customer service agents, or even ACPR/Banque de France officials to obtain authentication credentials or authorise fraudulent transfers. This attack exploits knowledge-based authentication (mother's maiden name, date of birth, last four digits of account number) that is insufficient in the modern threat environment. Biometric voice authentication for telephone banking — VerfiX Voice Biometrics — addresses this specifically by ensuring that the person calling the bank is the genuine account holder, regardless of what identity information they can provide verbally.

Identité synthétique et deepfakes

AI-generated synthetic identities and deepfake attacks on digital onboarding channels are a growing threat in the French market. The ACPR has specifically highlighted artificial intelligence-generated fraud as an emerging supervisory concern. VerfiX Fraud Intelligence includes dedicated deepfake detection, synthetic identity analysis, and document fraud signals tuned for the French and European document landscape. Section 8

RGPD — Data Protection for French KYC

RGPD (Règlement général sur la protection des données) — the French name for GDPR — governs the processing of personal data in France. France's national data protection authority, the CNIL (Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés), is one of the most active GDPR supervisory authorities in Europe, having issued several high-profile enforcement actions including major fines against global technology companies.

For biometric KYC processing, RGPD Article 9 classifies biometric data as a special category of personal data requiring an explicit legal basis. For LCB-FT-regulated entities, the statutory identification obligation under CMF Art. L.561-5 provides the legal basis under RGPD Art. 6(1)(c) (legal obligation) and Art. 9(2)(g) (substantial public interest established by law). The CNIL has published specific guidance on biometric processing in the financial sector.

CNIL Requirements for Biometric KYC

DPA Obligatoire (Art. 28)

Contrat de sous-traitance mandatory with all processors. CNIL actively enforces DPA requirements — including against French financial institutions.

Minimisation des données (Art. 5)

Biometric templates must be deleted after verification — not retained indefinitely. Only the non-biometric verification outcome is retained for LCB-FT purposes.

AIPD requise (Art. 35)

Analyse d'impact relative à la protection des données (AIPD) required for large-scale biometric processing. CNIL provides a methodology and expects formal AIPD documentation.

Notification CNIL (Art. 33)

Breaches likely to result in risk to data subjects must be notified to CNIL within 72 hours. France has one of the highest breach notification rates in the EU.

Confidentialité dès la conception

Privacy by design (Art. 25) requires privacy-protective defaults from system deployment. Biometric data deletion at session end must be the default configuration.

Switzerland holds EU RGPD/GDPR adequacy status. No SCCs required for processing French customer data in Swiss infrastructure via VerfiX.

The CNIL has specifically addressed AI-based facial recognition and biometric identity verification in its guidelines. Financial institutions must conduct an AIPD before deploying biometric KYC and must ensure that the legal basis, processing purposes, retention periods, and data subject rights are documented before the first verification. Section 9

Common French KYC Compliance Challenges

Based on ACPR inspection findings, FATF evaluation reports, and engagement with French financial institutions, the following challenges are consistently identified in French LCB-FT compliance programmes:

01
Classification du risque insuffisamment granulaire

Many French institutions implement binary risk classification (standard/enhanced) rather than a granular, documented risk score per customer. ACPR expects institutions to demonstrate proportionality between assessed risk and the intensity of due diligence applied — requiring a defensible, documented risk classification methodology.

02
RBE non consulté systématiquement

Consultation of the Registre des bénéficiaires effectifs is required but often performed inconsistently — done for some corporate customers but not others, or consulted but not documented. ACPR inspections specifically target UBO identification quality and RBE consultation evidence.

03
Surveillance continue ad hoc

French institutions frequently perform sanctions screening at onboarding and then rely on infrequent manual rescreening rather than genuine ongoing monitoring. Given that the EU and French national lists are updated frequently, point-in-time screening at onboarding is insufficient. ACPR expects demonstrated continuous monitoring.

04
Déclarations de soupçon sous-déclarées

France's SAR volume is high but ACPR consistently identifies under-reporting in specific sectors — particularly among non-bank financial intermediaries and DNFBPs. The obligation to file is triggered by suspicion, not certainty. Institutions with low SAR rates relative to their risk profile attract supervisory attention.

05
Données RGPD non conformes pour la biométrie

Institutions deploying biometric KYC frequently lack the required AIPD, have not executed a compliant DPA with their biometric processing vendor, or retain biometric data beyond the purpose-limited session. CNIL enforcement in financial services is increasing.

06
Onboarding numérique non homologué

Some French institutions use digital onboarding flows that do not fully meet CMF Art. L.561-5-1 requirements — lacking liveness detection, using insufficient document verification, or generating inadequate audit trails. ACPR's joint guidance with AMF sets clear minimum standards that must be documented and demonstrated.

France KYC — Questions fréquentes

Réponses aux questions les plus fréquentes sur les obligations KYC en France, la réglementation ACPR et la conformité LCB-FT.

KYC — Know Your Customer, or in French Connaissance du Client (KYC/CDD) — is the statutory obligation for French financial institutions and designated non-financial businesses to verify customer identity, assess money laundering risk, and maintain ongoing documentation under the Code monétaire et financier (CMF). In France, AML/KYC obligations are enforced by the ACPR (Autorité de contrôle prudentiel et de résolution) for the financial sector and by TRACFIN for financial intelligence. Failure to comply is subject to ACPR sanctions including fines of up to EUR 100 million or 10% of annual revenue.

The ACPR (Autorité de contrôle prudentiel et de résolution) is the French supervisory authority responsible for banking and insurance. It operates under the Banque de France and enforces AML/CFT obligations for regulated financial entities. ACPR conducts on-site inspections, issues binding orders, and imposes sanctions via its Commission des sanctions. ACPR also issues supervisory guidelines (lignes directrices) that clarify GwG-equivalent obligations under the Code monétaire et financier. ACPR works alongside the AMF (Autorité des marchés financiers) for investment services and MiCA crypto supervision.

LCB-FT (Lutte contre le blanchiment de capitaux et le financement du terrorisme) is the French AML/CFT framework, implemented in the Code monétaire et financier (CMF) Articles L.561-1 to L.574-2. It transposes EU AML Directives (AMLD4, AMLD5, AMLD6) into French law and is aligned with FATF Recommendations. The framework covers customer due diligence, beneficial owner identification, ongoing monitoring, suspicious activity reporting to TRACFIN, and internal AML programme requirements.

TRACFIN (Traitement du renseignement et action contre les circuits financiers clandestins) is France's Financial Intelligence Unit, operating under the Ministry of Economy. When a regulated entity identifies a suspicious transaction or activity, it must file a Déclaration de soupçon (DS) with TRACFIN via a secure online portal. France receives over 170,000 suspicious activity declarations annually. TRACFIN analyses reports, shares intelligence with law enforcement, and prosecutes predicate offences through the parquet national financier (PNF).

FranceConnect is the French government's digital identity federation, allowing citizens to use existing government credentials (tax authority, social security, postal service) to authenticate with public services. FranceConnect+ provides a higher assurance level using certified identity providers including La Poste's digital identity service. For KYC purposes, FranceConnect+ provides government-assured identity verification that ACPR accepts for certain use cases. VerfiX eID Gateway supports FranceConnect+ integration alongside EU Digital Identity Wallet acceptance.

Under CMF Article L.561-12, French financial institutions must retain all customer due diligence records — including identity documents or copies, beneficial owner declarations, and transaction records — for five years after the end of the business relationship. Suspicious activity declarations to TRACFIN are retained for five years from the declaration date. ACPR inspectors examine retention practices and the accessibility of records during supervisory reviews.

The Registre des bénéficiaires effectifs (RBE) is France's beneficial ownership register, maintained by the Greffe du tribunal de commerce (commercial court registry). All French legal entities must register their beneficial owners (persons holding more than 25% of shares or voting rights) and update this information when it changes. Financial institutions must consult the RBE as part of their KYB process and document any discrepancies between register data and customer-provided information.
